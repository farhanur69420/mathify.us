01 — FOUNDATION

What is Traffic Engineering?

Traffic Engineering is the branch of transportation engineering that focuses on the safe, efficient movement of people and vehicles. It covers road design, signal timing, intersection control, and flow analysis.

Think of the entire road network as a city's blood circulatory system. Roads are the arteries. Vehicles are blood cells. Traffic lights are valves. Too many cells (congestion) causes blockage. Traffic engineers are the cardiologists who keep the system healthy.
🛣️

Mobility

Moving efficiently from A to B at speed. Freeways maximize this — no stops, no driveways, no signals.

High speed → Low access
🏠

Accessibility

Being able to pull in and out of properties. Local streets maximize this — driveways everywhere.

Low speed → High access
⚖️

The Tradeoff

You can't have both at once. More access points = more friction = less mobility. This is the fundamental tension in road design.

The Principal Goal

Traffic Engineers design systems that provide a safe and efficient environment for roadway/highway traffic — minimizing crashes, delays, and fuel waste simultaneously.
02 — HIGHWAY FUNCTIONAL CLASSIFICATION

Road Hierarchy — Not All Roads Are Equal

Roads are classified by their function — what job they do in the network. The classification determines design speed, access controls, and who uses them.

Think of the road system like an airport terminal. Freeways = runways (fast, no interruptions). Arterials = main terminal corridors (fast but with gates). Collectors = concourses (slower, connect to gates). Local streets = individual gates (slow, direct door access).

03 — TRAFFIC OPERATIONS: IMPORTANT TERMS

The Language of Traffic Flow

Four critical terms that look similar but mean different things. Getting these right is the foundation of every HCM analysis.

Imagine a coffee shop. Capacity = max customers per hour the baristas can serve. Demand = how many customers want to come. Volume = how many actually walked in this hour. Flow Rate = how fast they're arriving right now (the busy 15 minutes during morning rush).
🏋️

Capacity (c)

The maximum vehicles a section can handle under typical conditions. A ceiling — you can't exceed it physically.

veh/hr/lane — a fixed physical limit
🙋

Demand

How many vehicles want to use the road. Demand can exceed capacity — that's when queues form.

veh/hr — what drivers want to do
📊

Volume (V)

How many vehicles actually pass a point in a given time. Measured by counting. Equals demand if no congestion.

veh/hr or veh/day — measured count
⚡

Flow Rate (v)

Like volume but for a sub-hour period, expressed as if it lasted a full hour. Captures peak intensity.

v = V/PHF — the true peak rate

Peak Hour Factor (PHF) — Catching the Worst 15 Minutes

The full hour total might be 3,600 vehicles. But if 1,200 came in just one 15-minute window, you actually need to design for 4,800 veh/hr equivalent. PHF catches this spike. A PHF of 1.0 = perfectly even flow. PHF of 0.8 = significant peaking (the worst 15 min carries 25% more than average).
Peak Hour Factor
PHF = V / (4 × V₁₅,max)
V = hourly volume  |  V₁₅,max = max 15-minute count  |  Range: 0.25 to 1.00
Maximum Flow Rate
v = V / PHF
Convert hourly volume to equivalent flow rate. Use this for capacity analysis, not raw volume.

04 — AT-GRADE INTERSECTIONS

Where Roads Meet

An at-grade intersection is where two or more roads cross at the same level. The key challenge: multiple streams of vehicles want to use the same space at the same time — someone must yield.

An intersection is like a four-way conversation. Everyone can't talk simultaneously — someone facilitates (the signal) or people take turns (yield/stop control). Without any control, it's chaos (everyone talks over everyone = crashes).

Levels of Intersection Control

🆓

No Control

Drivers adjust speed on their own. Only suitable for very low volume, good sight distance intersections.

🔺

Yield

Minor road yields to major road. Driver must slow but can proceed if clear. Shared responsibility.

🛑

STOP

Minor road must fully stop before entering. Must wait for a safe gap in major road traffic.

🚦

Signal Control

ALL approaches stop when red. Controlled turns. Best for high-volume, multi-modal intersections.

Key Intersection Geometry Terms

Approach

Lane(s) through which vehicles enter the intersection. The demand side.

Departure

Where vehicles leave after the intersection. The output side.

Channelizing Island

Raised pavement that physically directs turning movements and protects pedestrians.

Crosswalk

Marked pedestrian crossing zone. Must be timed with signal phases for pedestrian safety.

05 — TRAFFIC SIGNALS & CONTROL DEVICES

Teaching Drivers to Share Space

Traffic signals are the most powerful tool for managing intersection conflicts. But they must be justified — signals aren't always the solution, and a poorly-timed signal can make things worse.

A traffic signal is like a speaking schedule in a meeting. It gives each group their turn, ensures everyone gets heard, and prevents interruptions. Install a signal where it's not needed? You've added unnecessary delays — like making people raise hands in a two-person chat.

Why Install a Signal? The 9 MUTCD Warrants

A signal should only be installed if at least one warrant is met. Over-signalization increases delay, frustration, and can increase rear-end crashes.

W1: 8-Hour Vehicular Volume

High consistent traffic volume throughout the day.

W2: 4-Hour Vehicular Volume

High volumes during multiple periods.

W3: Peak Hour

Excessive delay during peak hours only.

W4: Pedestrian Volume

High pedestrian demand crossing a busy road.

W5: School Crossing

Children crossing near a school.

W7: Crash Experience

Documented crash pattern that signals can reduce.

06 — SIGNAL TIMING DESIGN

The Rhythm of an Intersection

Signal timing is about efficiently allocating the most limited resource at an intersection: time. Every second given to one phase is a second taken from another.

Signal timing is like baking multiple dishes for a dinner party using one oven. Each dish (phase) needs oven time. The full dinner cycle repeats. You lose time switching between dishes (lost time). You want everyone done as fast as possible — that's optimal timing.

Key Timing Terms

Cycle Length (C)

Total time for ONE complete sequence of all phases (greens, yellows, reds). Typically 60–120 seconds.

Longer C → less lost time waste → more capacity

Phase (Φ)

Green + yellow + all-red for one movement. A 2-phase signal serves N-S then E-W.

Green Interval (G)

Actual green light duration for a phase. What you see on the signal head.

Effective Green (g)

The time during which traffic can actually flow at saturation rate. Slightly different from display green.

g = G + y + ar − tL

Lost Time (tL)

Wasted time in each phase: start-up (vehicles accelerating) + clearance (last yellow vehicles). Usually ~4 seconds/phase.

tL = l₁ + l₂ ≈ 4 sec

All-Red (AR)

Brief moment when ALL approaches are red. Safety clearance interval — allows intersection to clear.

Effective Green Time
g = G + y + ar − tL
G=display green   y=yellow   ar=all-red   tL=total lost time per phase (~4s)

07 — SATURATION FLOW RATE

How Fast Can Vehicles Actually Discharge?

When a queue of vehicles gets a green light, there's a maximum rate at which they can cross the stop bar. That's the saturation flow rate — the physical throughput limit of a lane.

Imagine a tube of toothpaste. The saturation flow rate is the maximum paste per second that can come out when you squeeze hard. Squeeze harder = same rate (it's saturated). The saturation headway is the time between each toothpaste "droplet" (vehicle) at that maximum rate.
Saturation Flow Rate
s = 3600 / h
s = saturation flow rate (veh/h/ln)  |  h = saturation headway (s/veh)  |  Typical h ≈ 2.0–2.5 s → s ≈ 1440–1800 veh/h
Typical values: For a standard approach lane, s ≈ 1,800 veh/h/ln is the baseline in HCM. Adjustments apply for heavy vehicles, grades, turning movements, etc.
08 — LANE GROUP CAPACITY & v/c RATIO

Capacity, Demand, and the X Factor

At a signalized intersection, a lane group doesn't get the full hour of green — it only gets a fraction (g/C). So its effective capacity is reduced proportionally.

If a factory can make 1,000 widgets/hour at full speed, but it's only allowed to run for 37 out of every 70 minutes — its effective capacity is 1,000 × (37/70) = 529 widgets per cycle-hour. Same logic for a lane group: full saturation rate × green ratio = actual capacity.
Lane Group Capacity
cᵢ = sᵢ × (gᵢ / C)
sᵢ=saturation flow   gᵢ=effective green   C=cycle length
v/c Ratio (X) — The Key Performance Indicator
X = v / c = vᵢ / [sᵢ × (gᵢ/C)]
X < 1.0 → lane group OK (demand < capacity). X = 1.0 → at capacity. X > 1.0 → FAILURE — demand exceeds supply, queue grows indefinitely.

09 — CONTROL DELAY & LEVEL OF SERVICE

How Bad is the Wait? The LOS System

Level of Service (LOS) is the report card of an intersection. It grades performance from A (smooth, fast) to F (failed, gridlock). The metric is average control delay per vehicle (seconds).

LOS is like restaurant wait times. LOS A = walk-in, seated immediately. LOS B = 5-minute wait. LOS C = 20 min. LOS D = 45 min. LOS E = 1+ hour wait, people getting restless. LOS F = you're turned away, queue out the door.

Uniform Delay Formula (Webster / HCM)

Imagine vehicles arrive at a red light and form a queue. Uniform delay assumes they arrive at a perfectly steady rate. It calculates the average wait based on how long the red is and how much of the cycle is green.
Uniform Delay d₁
d₁ = 0.5C(1 − g/C)² / [1 − min(1,X)·(g/C)]
C=cycle length   g=effective green   X=v/c ratio   Result in seconds/vehicle
Total Control Delay
d = d₁ + d₂ + d₃
d₁=uniform delay   d₂=incremental (random arrival) delay   d₃=preexisting queue delay

10 — PUBLIC TRANSPORTATION

Moving Many People Efficiently

Public transportation moves people collectively, reducing the number of vehicles on roads. It ranges from taxis and buses all the way to fully grade-separated rail systems.

Public transit is like a shared rideshare vs. individual cars. One bus at 40 people = 38 fewer cars on the road. The tradeoff: less flexibility but huge capacity gains and environmental benefits.

11 — TRANSPORTATION PLANNING

Planning Before Building

Transportation planning is the process of deciding what infrastructure to build, where, when, and for whom — before spending billions of dollars. It requires predicting future travel demand.

Planning a transit system without demand forecasting is like opening a restaurant without checking if anyone wants to eat there. Travel demand forecasting tells you: how many people will use it, where they'll come from, and which route they'll take.

12 — STEP 1: TRIP GENERATION

Who Makes How Many Trips?

Trip generation predicts how many trips start or end in each zone based on zone characteristics (land use, population, employment). It answers: "how much demand will this zone generate?"

A mall attracts 50,000 trips/day. A residential neighborhood produces trips (people leave home for work/shopping). A school produces AND attracts trips (students leave home, arrive at school). Trip generation quantifies these flows.

Trip Purpose Categories

🏠→🏢 HBW (Home-Based Work)

Trips from home to workplace. Most time-sensitive, highest frequency. Drives morning/evening peak.

🏠→🛒 HBO (Home-Based Other)

Home to shopping, school, recreation. More flexible timing. Distributed throughout day.

🏢→🏢 NHB (Non-Home Based)

Neither end at home. Lunch trips, delivery runs, errands between stops.

Production vs Attraction
Production: trip end at RESIDENTIAL land use Attraction: trip end at NON-RESIDENTIAL land use
For NHB trips: Production = zone of origin, Attraction = zone of destination
Key constraint: Total zone productions must equal total zone attractions. If they don't balance, attractions are scaled: Aᵢ(adj) = Aᵢ × (ΣP / ΣA)
13 — STEP 2: TRIP DISTRIBUTION (GRAVITY MODEL)

Where Do Those Trips Actually Go?

Trip distribution connects origin zones to destination zones. More trips go to zones that are: (1) large (lots of attractions) and (2) nearby (low travel time). This is exactly Newton's Law of Gravity — applied to people.

Gravity analogy: Two cities attract each other based on their size (population/jobs) and inversely based on distance. A huge mall far away competes with a small mall nearby. The gravity model formalizes this intuition mathematically. More mass + less distance = more trips.
Gravity Model Formula
Tᵢⱼ = Pᵢ × [Aⱼ × F(t)ᵢⱼ × Kᵢⱼ] / [Σⱼ Aⱼ × F(t)ᵢⱼ × Kᵢⱼ]
Tᵢⱼ=trips from i to j   Pᵢ=productions at i   Aⱼ=attractions at j
F(t)ᵢⱼ=friction factor (function of travel time)   Kᵢⱼ=socioeconomic factor (usually 1.0)
F = 1/W^c where W=travel time, c=calibration parameter

14 — STEP 3: MODE SPLIT (LOGIT MODEL)

Car, Bus, or Train? The Logit Model

Mode split predicts what fraction of trips will use each available mode. It's based on the concept of utility — each mode has a perceived benefit/cost, and travelers (statistically) pick the highest utility option.

Think of choosing between coffee shops. The one closest to you is convenient (low travel time = high utility). The fancy one is expensive but better quality. You weigh cost vs. quality subconsciously. The logit model formalizes this weighting, predicting what fraction of people choose each option.
Utility Function
U = a₀ + a₁X₁ + a₂X₂ + ... + aᵣXᵣ
a₀=mode-specific constant   X₁=cost (cents)   X₂=travel time (min)   Coefficients are negative (more cost/time = lower utility)
Multinomial Logit — Probability of Choosing Mode i
P(i) = e^V(i) / Σ e^V(r)
V(i)=computed utility of mode i   Higher probability = more people choose this mode
