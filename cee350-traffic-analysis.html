01 — WHAT IS TRAFFIC ENGINEERING?

The Science of Making Roads Work

Traffic Engineering is the branch of transportation engineering that deals with planning, designing, and operating road networks so that vehicles move safely and efficiently. Think of traffic engineers as the people who decide: where signals go, how long each green light lasts, and how many lanes a road needs.

🧠 The Big AnalogyYour city's road network is a living organism's circulatory system. Roads = arteries & veins. Vehicles = blood cells. Intersections = heart valves. Traffic signals = the electrical pulses that open and close valves. Congestion = a blockage. Traffic engineers = cardiovascular surgeons keeping the whole system healthy and flowing.
🛣️

Mobility

Getting from A to B quickly. Freeways maximize this — no stops, no driveways, high speed.

High speed · Low access
🏠

Accessibility

The ability to enter/exit properties. Local streets maximize this — every driveway allowed.

Low speed · High access
⚖️

The Fundamental Tradeoff

More access = more friction = less mobility. You can't max out both. Road design is always a balance.

The central tension in road design
🎯

The Goal

Provide a safe and efficient system for all road users — vehicles, pedestrians, cyclists, and transit.

Safety + Efficiency always

Arterials = Main terminal corridors (fast but with security gates = signals). Collectors = Concourses (slower, connecting gates). Local streets = Individual gates (slowest, direct door access). Each has a job.

03 — THE FOUR MOST IMPORTANT TRAFFIC TERMS

Capacity, Demand, Volume, Flow Rate

These four terms look similar but mean totally different things. Getting them confused is the #1 mistake students make. Here's the breakdown, with analogies, then the math.

Term 01
Capacity (c)
The maximum number of vehicles that can pass over a section of roadway during a specified time period under prevailing conditions. It is a physical ceiling — you literally cannot push more cars through. Normally expressed as vehicles per hour per lane (veh/h/ln).
Symbol: c  |  Units: veh/h/ln  |  Typical freeway: ~2,000–2,400 veh/h/ln
🧠 Capacity AnalogyA water pipe. The pipe has a maximum flow rate — physics prevents you from pushing more water through no matter how hard you try. Cars are the same. A 10-foot-wide lane can only physically discharge so many vehicles per hour at highway speed.
Term 02
Demand
The number of vehicles that want to use a section of road during a specified time period. Demand can EXCEED capacity — that's what causes queues and backups. If 3,000 vehicles want to use a road with capacity 2,000, the excess 1,000 vehicles have to wait in a queue.
Key insight: Demand ≠ Volume when demand > capacity
Term 03
Volume (V)
The actual count of vehicles passing a point during a given time period. This is what a traffic counter measures. When demand ≤ capacity, Volume = Demand. When demand > capacity, Volume = Capacity (the queue is building, and you can only count what actually passes).
Symbol: V  |  Units: veh/h, veh/day, veh/15-min
Term 04
Flow Rate (v)
The rate of flow for a period shorter than one hour, but expressed as an equivalent hourly rate. You might count 300 vehicles in 15 minutes — that's a flow rate of 1,200 veh/h. This is what you use for capacity analysis because the peak 15 minutes drives congestion, not the whole hour average.
v = V / PHF  |  Always ≥ V (since PHF ≤ 1.0)
🧠 Coffee Shop Analogy for all 4 termsYou run a coffee shop. Capacity = your baristas can serve max 120 customers/hour. Demand = 200 people want to come during the morning rush. Volume = only 120 actually get served (the rest are queued outside). Flow Rate = during the busiest 15 minutes, 45 people came in → that's 180/hr equivalent — your true peak stress on the system.

05 — PEAK HOUR FACTOR (PHF)

The PHF: Catching Traffic Spikes

Traffic doesn't flow evenly within a single hour. Most of the action happens in a 15-minute burst. The Peak Hour Factor (PHF) measures HOW CONCENTRATED that peak is. A low PHF means traffic is very "peaky" — most vehicles arrive in a short burst.

🧠 Elevator AnalogyAn office has 1,000 workers leaving in one hour. If they leave evenly (250 every 15 min) → PHF = 1.0. If everyone rushes out at 5pm sharp (800 in first 15 min!) → PHF = 1000/(4×800) = 0.31. Low PHF = dangerous spike in demand. The elevator (road) must handle 800 people at once, not 250.
★ Peak Hour Factor Formula
PHF = V / (4 × V₁₅,max)
V = total hourly volume (veh/h)  ·  V₁₅,max = highest 15-minute count within the hour  ·  4 = converts 15-min period to hourly equivalent  ·  Range: 0.25 (very peaky) to 1.00 (perfectly uniform)
Maximum Flow Rate (what you use for capacity analysis)
v = V / PHF
This converts your hourly count to the equivalent peak rate. Always v ≥ V. Design for v, not V.

06 — AT-GRADE INTERSECTIONS

Where Roads Meet: Managing Conflict

An at-grade intersection is where two or more roads cross on the same level. The fundamental problem: multiple streams of vehicles want to occupy the same physical space at the same time. Without control, this is catastrophic.

🧠 Four-Way Conversation AnalogyAn intersection is a group conversation. Everyone wants to talk (drive through) at once. Without rules, everyone interrupts (crashes). A STOP sign = raise your hand and wait. A traffic signal = a moderator who says "OK, north-south group, you speak for 40 seconds. Now east-west group, your turn." Control = organized turn-taking.
🆓

No Control

Drivers adjust speed and negotiate gaps on their own. Only safe at very low volumes with great visibility.

🔺

Yield Control

Minor road traffic must yield (slow down, check for gaps) to major road. Still their choice when to go.

🛑

STOP Control

Must come to a complete stop. Must wait for a safe gap in major road traffic. More restrictive than yield.

🚦

Signal Control

Traffic signal assigns right-of-way. Everyone stops when they get red. Most controlled — best for high volumes.

Key terms: The functional area of an intersection extends upstream (where vehicles decelerate and queue) and downstream (where they accelerate back to speed). This is larger than the physical pavement area you can see. The physical area is just where the pavements overlap.

A traffic signal doesn't just tell you to stop and go — it's a precision timing instrument allocating a scarce resource (time in the intersection) to competing demands. Every second matters.

The controller (signal) gives each flight (movement) a specific time window to use the runway. A missed timing or overlap = disaster. Precision is everything.

Why Install a Signal? The 9 MUTCD Warrants

⚠️ CRITICAL: A signal should ONLY be installed if at least one of the 9 warrants is met. An unneeded signal INCREASES average delay and frustration, and can increase rear-end crash rates by 20–30%.

W1: 8-Hour Vehicular Volume

High, consistent traffic both on the main road AND the cross street during 8+ hours of the day.

W2: 4-Hour Vehicular Volume

Similar to W1 but for 4 hours — for moderately busy intersections.

W3: Peak Hour

Unacceptable delay for minor road vehicles during peak hour even if 8-hour volumes don't qualify.

W4: Pedestrian Volume

High pedestrian crossings on a busy road where gaps in traffic are rare.

W5: School Crossing

Children need to cross a busy arterial near a school — high vulnerability justifies signal.

W7: Crash Experience

Documented crash pattern (5+ injury or PDO crashes in 12 months) that a signal could reduce.

08 — SIGNAL TIMING: EVERY TERM EXPLAINED

The Anatomy of a Signal Cycle

Signal timing is the art of allocating TIME — the intersection's most scarce resource — to competing vehicle movements. Master these terms and every signal problem becomes solvable.

🧠 Shared Oven AnalogyYou're cooking 4 dishes (phases) but have ONE oven (intersection). Each dish needs a specific amount of oven time (green). You lose time when you switch between dishes — the oven heats back up, food adjusts (lost time). The total cooking session from first dish to starting over = one cycle. You want everyone done as fast as possible with minimum waste.
TIMING TERM 1
Cycle Length (C)
The total time for ONE complete sequence of all signal phases. If Phase 1 = 40s green + 3s yellow + 2s all-red, and Phase 2 = 20s green + 3s yellow + 2s all-red → Cycle = 40+3+2+20+3+2 = 70 seconds. The cycle repeats over and over.
Typical range: 60–120 seconds  |  Longer C = more throughput but more delay per vehicle
TIMING TERM 2
Phase (Φ)
A phase is the combination of movements that get right-of-way simultaneously. In a basic 2-phase signal: Phase 1 = north-south through + turn movements. Phase 2 = east-west movements. Each phase includes its green, yellow, and all-red interval.
A simple intersection = 2 phases. Complex (with protected lefts) = 4 phases. More phases = more lost time.
TIMING TERM 3
Green Interval (G)
The actual duration of the green light display for a phase. This is what you SEE on the signal head. However, this is NOT the same as effective green time — because there's startup delay at the beginning and some vehicles run through yellow at the end.
G = what the signal head shows  |  Different from g (effective green)
TIMING TERM 4 ★ KEY
Effective Green Time (g)
The time during which traffic can ACTUALLY flow at the saturation flow rate. It's slightly longer than display green because: (1) some vehicles clear during yellow and early all-red, but (2) time is lost at the start as drivers react and accelerate. Net effect: g ≈ G + y + ar - tL
g = G + y + ar − tL  |  This is what you use in ALL capacity calculations
TIMING TERM 5 ★ KEY
Lost Time (tL)
Time wasted during each phase change. Has two parts: (1) Start-up lost time (l₁) ≈ 2 seconds — the reaction/acceleration time of the first few vehicles when green turns on. (2) Clearance lost time (l₂) — time lost at end of yellow when flow rate drops below saturation. Total tL = l₁ + l₂ ≈ 4 seconds per phase.
tL = l₁ + l₂ ≈ 4 s/phase  |  With 2 phases: total lost time ≈ 8s per cycle (wasted!)
TIMING TERM 6
All-Red Interval (ar)
A brief period where ALL approaches simultaneously show red. It provides a safety clearance interval — allowing vehicles already in the intersection (ran the late yellow) to clear before the next phase gets green. Typically 1–3 seconds.
Duration: 1–3 seconds  |  Critical for pedestrian safety too
TIMING TERM 7
Protected vs Permitted Movement
Protected movement: Has dedicated right-of-way, doesn't yield to anyone (e.g., protected left turn arrow — oncoming traffic is stopped). Permitted movement: Must yield to conflicting flows — a left turner must wait for a gap in oncoming traffic even during a "green ball."
Protected = dedicated phase, guaranteed safe. Permitted = can go, but must yield to conflicts.
★ Effective Green Time Formula
g = G + y + ar − tL
G = display green duration  ·  y = yellow interval (typically 3–4s)  ·  ar = all-red interval (1–3s)  ·  tL = total lost time (≈4s/phase)  ·  Result = effective green in seconds

09 — SATURATION FLOW RATE

The Maximum Discharge Rate

When a queue of vehicles gets a green light, they don't all go at once — they discharge one by one. The maximum rate at which they flow once the queue is moving is called the saturation flow rate. It's the speed limit of vehicle throughput.

🧠 Toll Booth AnalogyImagine a toll booth that processes cars as fast as possible — one car every 2 seconds. That's a headway of 2 s/veh. The saturation flow rate = 3600 seconds/hour ÷ 2 seconds/vehicle = 1,800 vehicles/hour. That's the physical maximum the booth (lane) can process, regardless of how many cars are waiting.
KEY TERM
Saturation Headway (h)
The time gap between consecutive vehicles in a queue once they reach the saturation flow rate. Measured as seconds per vehicle. The first few cars in queue have LONGER headways (reaction + acceleration = startup lost time), but once the queue is "running," headways stabilize at h ≈ 2.0 seconds.
Typical h ≈ 2.0–2.5 s/veh for through lanes  |  Turning lanes have higher h (larger headway)
★ Saturation Flow Rate
s = 3600 / h
s = saturation flow rate (veh/h/ln)  ·  h = saturation headway (s/veh)  ·  3600 = seconds per hour  ·  Example: h=2.0s → s=1800 veh/h; h=2.5s → s=1440 veh/h

10 — LANE GROUP CAPACITY & v/c RATIO

How Much Can This Lane Actually Handle?

A lane can theoretically flow 1,800 vehicles per hour — but only if it's running green 100% of the time. In reality, it only gets a fraction of the cycle as green. That fraction (g/C) is multiplied by s to get the real capacity.

🧠 Factory AnalogyA factory machine can make 1,800 widgets/hour at full speed. But the factory only runs the machine for 37 out of every 70 minutes (g/C = 37/70 = 0.53). Effective production capacity = 1,800 × 0.53 = 954 widgets/hour. That's exactly how lane group capacity works — full saturation rate times the green ratio.
★ Lane Group Capacity
cᵢ = sᵢ × (gᵢ / C)
sᵢ = saturation flow rate (veh/h)  ·  gᵢ = effective green time (s)  ·  C = cycle length (s)  ·  gᵢ/C = green ratio (the key multiplier)
★ v/c Ratio (X) — The Key Performance Indicator
Xᵢ = vᵢ / cᵢ = vᵢ / [sᵢ × (gᵢ/C)]
vᵢ = demand flow rate (veh/h)  ·  cᵢ = capacity (veh/h)  ·  X < 1.0 = OK  ·  X = 1.0 = AT capacity  ·  X > 1.0 = FAILURE — queue grows indefinitely

11 — CONTROL DELAY & LEVEL OF SERVICE

Grading the Intersection: LOS A through F

Level of Service (LOS) is the "report card" for an intersection. It's based on average control delay per vehicle (seconds). The lower the delay, the better the LOS. LOS F means the intersection is failing — demand exceeds capacity.

🧠 Restaurant Wait Time AnalogyLOS is like restaurant wait times.

DELAY COMPONENTS
Total Control Delay = d₁ + d₂ + d₃
d₁ (Uniform delay): Assumes vehicles arrive at a perfectly steady rate. The primary delay component for most intersections. d₂ (Incremental delay): Accounts for random arrivals (bunching, platoons) and oversaturation. d₃ (Initial queue delay): Additional delay if there was already a queue at the start of the analysis period (carryover from previous cycles).
★ Uniform Delay (Webster / HCM Formula)
d₁ = 0.5C(1 − g/C)² / [1 − min(1,X)·(g/C)]
C = cycle length (s)  ·  g = effective green (s)  ·  X = v/c ratio  ·  min(1,X) = caps at 1.0 to prevent negative denominator  ·  Result in seconds/vehicle
Physical intuition: The numerator 0.5C(1-g/C)² represents the area of the delay triangle — the "triangle" of queued vehicles over time. The denominator corrects for the fact that when X is high (near capacity), vehicles clear slower, increasing delay dramatically.

12 — TRANSPORTATION PLANNING: THE 4-STEP MODEL

Predicting Future Traffic Before Building Roads

Before spending billions on infrastructure, planners use mathematical models to predict how many people will travel, where they'll go, what mode they'll use, and which routes they'll take. The four-step model is the industry standard.

🧠 Netflix Recommendation AnalogyThe 4-step model is like Netflix predicting what you'll watch. Step 1 (Trip Gen): How many shows will you watch this week? Step 2 (Trip Dist): Which genres/titles? Step 3 (Mode Split): Netflix vs YouTube vs Disney+? Step 4 (Assignment): Which device will you use, which time slot? Each step uses data + math to predict behavior at scale.
1

Trip Generation

How many trips does each zone produce and attract? Based on land use, households, jobs.

→ P and A values
2

Trip Distribution

Which zones send trips to which other zones? Gravity model. Creates the O-D matrix.

→ O-D Matrix Tᵢⱼ
3

Mode Split

Car, bus, or train? Logit model based on utility. Which mode wins for each zone pair?

→ % by mode
4

Traffic Assignment

Which specific route? User Equilibrium assigns traffic until no driver can reduce travel time by switching.

→ Link volumes
TAZ (Traffic Analysis Zone): The basic geographic unit. The study area is divided into zones. Each zone has a centroid — an imaginary point from which all trips in that zone are assumed to originate/terminate. Zone sizes range from city blocks in downtown to large areas in suburbs. A metro area of 1 million people might have 700–800 TAZs.
13 — STEP 1: TRIP GENERATION

How Many Trips Does Each Zone Make?

Trip generation predicts how many trips start (productions) and end (attractions) in each zone. It's based on land use characteristics — a residential zone produces trips (people leave for work), a commercial zone attracts them.

🧠 Store AnalogyThink of each zone as either a store or a neighborhood. Neighborhoods PRODUCE trips (residents drive to work, school, shopping). Stores ATTRACT trips (people come TO them). A huge mall attracts 50,000 trips/day. A neighborhood of 1,000 homes produces maybe 8,000 trips/day. Trip generation quantifies this using land use data.
🏠→🏢

HBW: Home-Based Work

Trips from home to work and back. Most time-sensitive (drives morning/evening peaks). ~25% of all daily trips.

🏠→🛒

HBO: Home-Based Other

Home to school, shopping, recreation, medical. More flexible timing. ~45% of daily trips.

🏢→🍽️

NHB: Non-Home Based

Neither end at home. Lunch trips, deliveries, errands between stops. ~30% of daily trips.

Key Relationship
ΣPᵢ = ΣAⱼ (must balance!)
Total productions in study area must equal total attractions. If raw Aⱼ don't balance: Aⱼ(adj) = Aⱼ × (ΣP / ΣA)
14 — STEP 2: TRIP DISTRIBUTION (GRAVITY MODEL)

Where Do Trips Actually Go?

Once we know how many trips each zone makes, we need to figure out where they go. The gravity model says: more trips go to zones that are BIGGER (more attractions) and CLOSER (less travel time). This is Newton's Law of Gravity applied to people.

🧠 Gravity AnalogyNewton said two objects attract each other based on their masses divided by the square of distance. The gravity model says two zones send trips to each other based on their "trip mass" (attractions × productions) divided by the square of travel time. A huge mall far away competes against a small mall nearby. Mass pulls, distance repels.
★ Gravity Model
Tᵢⱼ = Pᵢ × (Aⱼ × Fᵢⱼ × Kᵢⱼ) / Σⱼ(Aⱼ × Fᵢⱼ × Kᵢⱼ)
Tᵢⱼ = trips from zone i to zone j  ·  Pᵢ = productions in zone i  ·  Aⱼ = attractions in zone j
Fᵢⱼ = friction factor (travel time impedance: F = 1/tᶜ)  ·  Kᵢⱼ = socioeconomic adjustment (often = 1.0)
The denominator normalizes so that Σⱼ Tᵢⱼ = Pᵢ (all productions are accounted for)
Friction Factor Formula
Fᵢⱼ = 1 / Wᶜᵢⱼ
W = travel time (impedance) between zones i and j  ·  c = calibration parameter (typically 2.0 in examples)

15 — STEP 3: MODE SPLIT (LOGIT MODEL)

Car, Bus, or Train? The Logit Model Decides

Mode split predicts what fraction of trips use each mode. It's based on utility theory: each mode has a perceived "value" (utility) based on cost, time, and comfort. People choose the highest utility mode (probabilistically).

🧠 Coffee Shop Choice AnalogyYou need coffee. Options: Starbucks (expensive, 10 min walk, great), Dunkin (cheap, 2 min walk, OK), office machine (free, 0 min, bad). You weigh cost vs. time vs. quality. Everyone makes slightly different decisions. The logit model predicts the fraction of people who'll choose each option based on the average utility weights in the population.
★ Utility Function
U = a₀ + a₁X₁ + a₂X₂ + ... + aᵣXᵣ
a₀ = mode-specific constant (intrinsic preference)  ·  X₁ = cost (cents)  ·  X₂ = travel time (min)  ·  Coefficients (a) are negative for bad attributes (cost, time)
★ Multinomial Logit — Probability
P(i) = e^V(i) / Σᵣ e^V(r)
V(i) = computed utility of mode i  ·  e = Euler's number (≈2.718)  ·  Σᵣ = sum over all modes  ·  ΣP = 1.0 always (probabilities sum to 100%)

★ COMPLETE FORMULA SHEET

Every Formula in CEE 350

Traffic Volume
Peak Hour Factor
PHF = V / (4 × V₁₅,max)
V = hourly vol | V₁₅,max = max 15-min count | Range 0.25–1.00
Traffic Volume
Maximum Flow Rate
v = V / PHF
Always v ≥ V. Use v for capacity analysis, not raw V.
Signal Timing
Effective Green Time
g = G + y + ar − tL
tL = l₁ + l₂ ≈ 4s/phase. y typically 3–4s. ar typically 1–3s.
Saturation Flow
Saturation Flow Rate
s = 3600 / h
h = saturation headway (s/veh). Typical h=2.0s → s=1800 veh/h/ln
Capacity Analysis
Lane Group Capacity
cᵢ = sᵢ × (gᵢ/C)
gᵢ/C = green ratio. This is the KEY multiplier — scales s by fraction of cycle that is effective green.
Performance
v/c Ratio X
Xᵢ = vᵢ / cᵢ = vᵢ/[sᵢ(gᵢ/C)]
X <1.0 = OK | X=1.0 = at capacity | X>1.0 = LOS F failure
Delay
Uniform Delay d₁
d₁ = 0.5C(1-g/C)² / [1-min(1,X)·g/C]
Result in s/veh. Primary component for most analyses.
Delay
Total Control Delay
d = d₁ + d₂ + d₃
d₂=incremental (random arrivals) | d₃=initial queue
LOS Thresholds
Control Delay → LOS
A:≤10 B:10-20 C:20-35 D:35-55 E:55-80 F:>80
Units: seconds/vehicle. X>1.0 automatically = LOS F.
Pedestrian Timing
Min Pedestrian Green (WE>10ft)
Gp = 3.2 + (2.7·Nped/WE) + L/Sp
For WE≤10ft: Gp = 3.2 + 0.27·Nped + L/Sp
Trip Distribution
Gravity Model
Tᵢⱼ = Pᵢ[AⱼFᵢⱼKᵢⱼ] / Σ[AⱼFᵢⱼKᵢⱼ]
Fᵢⱼ = 1/Wᶜ | Kᵢⱼ = socioeconomic factor (usually 1.0)
Mode Split
Logit Model
P(i) = e^V(i) / Σe^V(r)
V = a₀ + Σaᵣ·Xᵣ | Coefficients negative for bad attributes
