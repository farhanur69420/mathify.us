00 — LEC 1 — FOUNDATION

Why Reinforced Concrete?

Concrete alone is like chalk — strong in compression, snaps in tension. Steel does the opposite. The primary materials to build a structure are steel and concrete, and together they form a near-perfect team.

🪨

Concrete in Compression

Concrete is an artificial stone made of cement, sand, water, and air. Like stone, it handles compression extremely well — loads pushing down on it.

f'c = 3,000 – 5,000 psi
⚠️

Concrete in Tension

Concrete is terrible at handling tension. Its tensile strength is roughly 1/10 of its compressive strength. It cracks easily under any bending load.

ft ≈ f'c / 10
🔩

Steel Takes the Tension

Steel bars (rebars) are placed in the tension zone of a concrete beam. They carry the tensile forces concrete cannot handle. Perfect teamwork.

fy = 60 ksi (Grade 60)
🔗

Bond — The Secret

The bond between steel and concrete is very strong. They deform together (strain compatibility) which is the fundamental assumption of RC design.

n = Es/Ec ≈ 8
Intuition First: Imagine bending a ruler. The top edge gets shorter (compression) and the bottom edge gets longer (tension). Now imagine the ruler is concrete — it snaps on the tension side. RC places steel exactly where the snapping would happen.

What Is Concrete Made Of?

Concrete consists of four components. Understanding each one explains its behavior:

🌫️

Air

Small voids left after mixing. Too much air reduces strength.

🟫

Cement

Adhesive (bonds with other materials) + Cohesive (bonds with itself). Portland cement is standard.

💧

Water

Causes hydration. w/c ratio controls workability vs strength.

🏖️

Sand (FA)

Fine Aggregate. Fills voids between coarse aggregate particles.

A Brief History

ANCIENT ROME

Pozzolana Cement

Romans used volcanic ash (pozzolana) mixed with lime. Used in the Pantheon dome — still standing 2,000 years later.

1796 — ENGLAND

Portland Cement — Joseph Aspdin

Joseph Aspdin patents Portland cement — the same type used in concrete worldwide today.

1832

First Concrete House

Plain concrete used for buildings. Immediately clear it struggles with tension — walls crack.

1867 — THE KEY DATE

Reinforced Concrete — Joseph Monier

French gardener Joseph Monier patents iron-reinforced concrete flower pots. The idea spreads rapidly to beams, slabs, and eventually skyscrapers.

01 — LEC 2 — MIX DESIGN & UNIT WEIGHTS

Concrete Composition: The Numbers

Before analyzing a beam, an engineer needs to know exactly what they're working with. Mix design defines the recipe — and the recipe determines the strength.

Coarse Aggregate (CA)

Coarse aggregate makes up 70–75% of concrete by volume. The standard maximum size in structural RC is ¾" (downgraded) — meaning the highest size stone chip is ¾". This is specified by BNBC and ACI.

What does "downgraded" mean? If the maximum aggregate size is listed as ¾", it means stone chips are sized down so no particle exceeds ¾". This matters for rebar spacing — aggregate must fit between bars.

Concrete Curing — 28 Days

28-Day Hardening Point: Concrete strength is tested and specified at 28 days. This is the "hardening point" — the reference age for all design values. f'c = 4000 psi means the cylinder test at 28 days gives 4000 psi. Before 28 days, concrete is still gaining strength.
02 — LEC 3 — LOADS & ACI SAFETY FRAMEWORK

What Loads Act on a Structure? Safety Provisions

Before designing, you must know what your structure must resist. ACI 318 defines a safety framework that ensures the structure's capacity always exceeds demand.

Three Types of Loads

DEAD LOAD (DL)
Permanent loads — the weight of the structure itself. Wall: γbrick = 120 pcf. RCC slab/beam: 150 pcf. Table 1.1 (BNBC) gives unit weights. Does not vary with time.
LIVE LOAD (LL)
Occupancy and use loads — people, furniture, equipment. Varies with time and use. Values from Table 1.1 (BNBC / ASCE 7). Example: Office = 50 psf, Residential = 40 psf.
ENVIRONMENTAL
Wind, earthquake (seismic), snow, rain, thermal. In Bangladesh: wind and seismic are primary environmental loads. Governed by BNBC.
1 pcf = 1 lb/ft³ — This is a unit weight. Multiply by volume to get total weight, which becomes the dead load force.

ACI Safety Provisions: Design Strength ≥ Required Strength

The fundamental ACI safety inequality. A structure must be able to carry more than it needs to:

ACI SAFETY FRAMEWORK
\[ \text{Design Strength} \geq \text{Required Strength} \] \[ \phi \cdot M_n \geq M_u \qquad \phi \cdot V_n \geq V_u \qquad \phi \cdot P_n \geq P_u \]
φ = Strength Reduction Factor (accounts for material variability and construction imperfection)
Mn = Nominal Moment Capacity (what the beam can theoretically carry)
Mu = Required (Ultimate) Moment (factored loads from analysis)
📐

Required Strength (Mu, Vu, Pu)

Determined by structural analysis. The largest bending moment among all load combinations (ACI specifies 20 possible combinations). The controlling one governs design.

Mu = max of 20 combos
🔵

Nominal Capacity (Mn, Vn, Pn)

Theoretical maximum strength of the section calculated from material properties and geometry. This is what we calculate in design.

Mn = Nominal BM Capacity
🛡️

Strength Reduction Factor (φ)

Applied to nominal capacity to account for uncertainty. φ < 1.0. For flexure: φ = 0.90. For shear: φ = 0.75. Provides a safety margin.

φflex = 0.90
Required Strength is the highest of 8 possible bending moments calculated from different load combinations. Structural analysis will determine the controlling moment Mu, shear Vu, and axial force Pu.

Prestressed Concrete — A Sidenote

Prestressed concrete is an advanced form of RC where high-strength steel tendons are deliberately stressed before load is applied, putting the entire concrete section into compression. This eliminates tensile cracking. Used for long spans (bridges, parking garages).

Not covered in this course — but understanding basic RC thoroughly is the prerequisite for understanding prestressed concrete.

\[ E_c = 57{,}000\sqrt{f'_c} \quad \text{(psi)} \]

\[ f_r = 7.5\sqrt{f'_c} \quad \text{(psi)} \]
This is the tensile stress at which concrete first cracks. Below this → section is uncracked (Stage I). Above this → section cracks and steel activates (Stage II).

Why Steel Is Isotropic — And Concrete Isn't

Steel — Isotropic

Steel has the same stress-strain curve in all directions (tension, compression, horizontal, vertical). This is called isotropic behavior. Es = 29,000 ksi in every direction.

Concrete — Anisotropic

Concrete behaves very differently in compression (strong) vs tension (weak). It is not isotropic. This is exactly why the bending stress formula σ = My/I cannot be directly applied to RC — it assumes isotropy.

Modular Ratio Calculator

The modular ratio n tells us how much stiffer steel is compared to concrete. It transforms a two-material problem into one material.

MODULAR RATIO
\[ n = \frac{E_s}{E_c} = \frac{29 \times 10^6}{57{,}000\sqrt{f'_c}} \]
n tells us: "1 in² of steel is equivalent to n in² of concrete." Typically n ≈ 6–10 for common concrete strengths. Steel is n times stiffer than concrete.

04 — LEC 4–5 — BEAM GEOMETRY & CROSS-SECTION

Beam Cross-Section: Cover, Depth, and Bars

Before calculating stresses, you need to define the beam geometry precisely. The effective depth d — not total height h — is what governs all calculations.

Why Cover Matters

Concrete cover protects steel from corrosion and fire. The minimum cover distance is specified by ACI and BNBC:

🔲

Clear Cover (Bottom)

Distance from the bottom face of the beam to the nearest steel bar surface. Protects against corrosion from below.

= 1.5" (standard) → 1" downgraded = 1.5" clearance
⬛

Side Cover

Distance from the side face of the beam to the nearest bar. Typically the same as clear cover.

= 1.5" (standard)
🌊

Underground / Offshore

For structures in contact with soil, water, or aggressive environments — increased cover is required.

= 2.5" to 3"
↔️

Clear Distance Between Bars

If CA = ¾" downgraded (max stone = ¾"), then clear distance between bars must be ≥ 1" (greater than the aggregate size).

≥ 1" (for ¾" CA)

Beam Width Conventions (BNBC)

BNBC Requirement: Beam width b ≥ 12". Always use even numbers: b = 12", 14", 16", 18", 20". This makes formwork economical and standardizes construction.

Effective Depth — The Key Dimension

The effective depth d is measured from the compression face to the centroid of the tension steel. This is what goes into every stress calculation.

BEAM CROSS-SECTION — DIMENSIONS
EFFECTIVE DEPTH FORMULA
\[ d = h - \text{clear cover} - d_{stirrup} - \frac{d_b}{2} \]
h = total beam height
clear cover = 1.5" (standard)
d_stirrup = stirrup bar diameter (typically 0.375" for #3 stirrups)
db = main bar diameter
Example: h = 18", 3-#8 bars (db = 1"), no stirrups
d = 18 − 1.5 − 0 − 1/2 = 16.0"
Example: h = 20", 6-#6 bars (db = 0.75"), CA = ¾"
d = 20 − 1.5 − 0.75/2 − 1.5" = 17.25"

Reinforcement Ratio ρ (rho)

The reinforcement ratio tells you how much steel you have relative to the beam size:

REINFORCEMENT RATIO
\[ \rho = \frac{A_s}{b \cdot d} \]
ρ↓ (low) → Lightly reinforced. Flexural failure governs. Section ductile.
ρ↑ (high) → Heavily reinforced. Concrete crushing may govern. Less ductile.
Example: 3 #8 bars, b=12", d=16": ρ = 3×0.79/(12×16) = 0.0123

Bar Arrangement — The Grid Rule

Bar centroid at center of the steel grid: When bars are in multiple rows, d is measured to the centroid of the entire steel group — not to any single bar. For bars in two rows: find the weighted centroid. This slightly reduces the effective depth d.
05 — LEC 6–7 — BENDING STRESS & TRANSFORMED SECTION

The Flexure Formula & Its Limits

When a beam bends, stresses vary linearly from top to bottom — compression on one side, tension on the other. But this formula has critical limitations for RC.

BENDING (FLEXURE) STRESS FORMULA
\[ \sigma = \frac{M \cdot y}{I} \]
σ = bending stress at distance y from neutral axis
M = bending moment at the section
y = distance from neutral axis to the point of interest
I = moment of inertia of the cross-section
⚠️ RC Limitation: This formula was derived for materials that are homogeneous (same throughout) and isotropic (same in all directions). Reinforced concrete is neither. We need special treatment — the Transformed Section method.

Why the Formula Fails for RC — Three Problems

Problem 1: Two Materials

RC has both concrete (low E) and steel (high E) in the same cross-section. The formula assumes one material with one E value.

Problem 2: Concrete Cracks

After cracking, the tension zone of concrete is void — it contributes nothing. The formula assumes a continuous solid cross-section.

Problem 3: Non-linearity

At high loads, concrete stress-strain is non-linear. The formula assumes linear elastic behavior throughout.

Solution: The Transformed Section

Since steel is n times stiffer than concrete, we replace the steel bars with an equivalent area of concrete. This creates a single-material "transformed section" we can analyze normally using σ = My/I.

ACTUAL RC SECTION
→
TRANSFORMED SECTION
TRANSFORMED STEEL AREA
\[ A_{s,\text{tr}} = n \cdot A_s \]
Replace each steel bar with n times its area in concrete. Now treat the whole section as one concrete material and apply σ = My/I normally.
FINDING THE NEUTRAL AXIS (Uncracked Transformed Section)

Take moments of area about the neutral axis. For an uncracked section, use (n-1)As if the steel occupies the same space as concrete:

\[ \bar{y} = \frac{b \cdot h \cdot h/2 + (n-1) A_s \cdot d}{b \cdot h + (n-1) A_s} \]
ȳ = depth to neutral axis from top. For a cracked section, use the quadratic form shown in Stage II analysis.
Q.0 Uncracked Transformed Section — Find stresses & check for cracking

GIVEN

Span:L = 20 ft, simply supported
Load:w = 0.75 k/ft (uniform)
Beam:b = 12", h = 18"
Steel:3 - #7 bars (As = 3 × 0.60 = 1.80 in²)
f'c:4,000 psi, fy = 60 ksi

Compute section properties

Effective depth and modular ratio:

d = 18 − 1.5 − 0 − 0.875/2 = 16.06" ≈ 16.1"
Ec = 57,000√4000 = 3,605,000 psi; n = 29,000,000/3,605,000 ≈ 8

Locate the neutral axis (uncracked, full section)

Taking moments about the top fiber:

ȳ = [bh·(h/2) + (n-1)As·d] / [bh + (n-1)As]
= [12×18×9 + 7×1.80×16.1] / [12×18 + 7×1.80]
= [1944 + 202.9] / [216 + 12.6] = 2146.9/228.6 = 9.39" from top

Moment of inertia (transformed, uncracked)

I_tr = bh³/12 + bh(ȳ − h/2)² + (n-1)As(d − ȳ)²
= 12×18³/12 + 12×18×(9.39−9)² + 7×1.80×(16.1−9.39)²
= 5832 + 12.6 + 568 = 6,412 in⁴

Maximum bending moment

M = wL²/8 = 0.75 × 20² / 8 = 37.5 k·ft = 450 k·in = 450,000 lb·in

Tensile stress at bottom fiber

y_bottom = h − ȳ = 18 − 9.39 = 8.61" (distance from N.A. to bottom)
f_ct = My/I = 450,000 × 8.61 / 6,412 = 604 psi

Check against modulus of rupture

fr = 7.5√4000 = 7.5 × 63.25 = 474 psi
f_ct = 604 psi > fr = 474 psi → Section IS cracked → Use Stage II analysis
Conclusion: Even at this relatively small load, the tensile stress (604 psi) exceeds the modulus of rupture (474 psi). The section has cracked. We must switch to Stage II (cracked section) analysis. This is exactly where the k & j method begins.
06 — LEC 6 — THREE STAGES OF LOADING

From Zero Load to Collapse

An RC beam doesn't fail suddenly. It goes through three distinct stages as load increases — and understanding each stage is the foundation of all RC analysis.

STAGE I

Section Uncracked

Tensile stress in concrete is below the modulus of rupture (fr). The full cross-section — concrete and steel together — resists the bending moment. Linear elastic behavior.

fct < fr = 7.5√f'c
STAGE II

Section Cracked (Elastic)

Tensile stress exceeds fr — concrete cracks in tension zone. Concrete in tension is ignored. Steel carries all tension. This is the WSD working range.

fs < fy , fc < ½f'c
STAGE III

Ultimate / Failure

Steel yields (reaches fy) or concrete crushes (εu = 0.003). Non-linear behavior. This is the basis of Strength Design (USD), not covered in WSD.

fs = fy or εc = 0.003

The Cracking Moment

Check tensile stress using bending formula

Using the uncracked transformed section, compute the tensile stress at the extreme bottom fiber:

\( f_{ct} = \frac{M \cdot y_{bottom}}{I_{tr}} \)

Compare to modulus of rupture

If fct < fr: Section is uncracked → Stage I analysis.
If fct > fr: Section has cracked → Stage II analysis.

\( f_r = 7.5\sqrt{f'_c} \quad \text{(psi)} \)

After cracking: ignore concrete tension

In Stage II, concrete cannot carry any tension at all. Only the compression zone above the neutral axis carries load, and steel carries all tension.

After cracking: fct = 0 (concrete tension = zero)
07 — LEC 7–8 — CRACKED SECTION ANALYSIS (WSD)

The k & j Method

Once a section is cracked (Stage II), two key dimensionless parameters govern everything: k (neutral axis ratio) and j (moment arm ratio).

CRACKED SECTION — FORCES
COMPRESSION FORCE (in concrete)
\[ C = \frac{1}{2} f_c \cdot b \cdot kd \]
TENSION FORCE (in steel)
\[ T = A_s \cdot f_s \]
MOMENT EQUILIBRIUM
\[ M = T \cdot jd = A_s \cdot f_s \cdot jd \] \[ M = C \cdot jd = \tfrac{1}{2} f_c \cdot b \cdot kd \cdot jd \]
Key idea: Equilibrium tells us C = T (no net horizontal force). With a known moment M, we can find both the concrete stress fc and steel stress fs. WSD checks that both remain below their allowable values.

Define the reinforcement ratio ρ (rho)

\( \rho = \frac{A_s}{b \cdot d} \)

Force equilibrium: C = T

\( \frac{1}{2} f_c \cdot b \cdot kd = A_s \cdot f_s \quad \Rightarrow \quad \rho \cdot f_s = \frac{1}{2} f_c \cdot k \)

Strain compatibility (linear strain diagram)

\( k = \frac{n \cdot f_c}{n \cdot f_c + f_s} \)

Solve the quadratic for k

\( k^2 + 2\rho n \cdot k - 2\rho n = 0 \)
\( \boxed{k = \sqrt{(\rho n)^2 + 2\rho n} - \rho n} \)

The moment arm ratio j

\( \boxed{j = 1 - \frac{k}{3}} \)
This comes from the centroid of the triangular stress block being at kd/3 from the top.
STRESSES — SOLVING FOR fc AND fs
\[ f_s = \frac{M}{A_s \cdot j \cdot d} \qquad f_c = \frac{2M}{k \cdot j \cdot b \cdot d^2} \]

GIVEN

Span:L = 20 ft, simply supported
Load:w = 4 k/ft (uniform)
Width:b = 12"
Height:h = 18"
Steel:6 #6 bars → As = 6 × 0.44 = 2.64 in²

Section properties

d = 18 − 1.5 − 0 − 6/8 − 0.5 = 15.25" (with #6 bars, 3/4" dia)

ρ and n

ρ = 2.64/(12 × 15.25) = 0.0144
n = 29×10⁶ / (57,000√4000) = 8.0
ρn = 0.0144 × 8 = 0.115

k and j

k = √(0.115² + 2×0.115) − 0.115 = 0.378

08 — LEC 9+ — WORKING STRESS DESIGN (WSD)

Designing an RC Beam

WSD limits stresses to safe fractions of the material strengths. The allowable stresses are set at half of failure — giving a safety factor of 2.

🔵

Allowable Concrete Stress

In WSD, concrete compression is limited to half its compressive strength to stay in the linear elastic range with an adequate safety factor.

fc,allow = ½ f'c
🟡

Allowable Steel Stress

Steel stress is limited to half its yield strength — ensuring we stay well within the linear elastic range.

fs,allow = ½ fy
WSD DESIGN EQUATIONS
\[ bd^2 = \frac{2M}{k \cdot j \cdot f_c} \qquad A_s = \frac{M}{f_s \cdot j \cdot d} \]
where fc = ½f'c and fs = ½fy are the allowable stresses
Q.1.B Design cross-section for w = 4 k/ft, L = 20 ft

GIVEN

Load:w = 4 k/ft, L = 20 ft
f'c:4,000 psi → fc,allow = ½(4000) = 2,000 psi
fy:60,000 psi → fs,allow = ½(60,000) = 30,000 psi

Compute k from allowable stresses (n = 8)

k = n·fc / (n·fc + fs) = 8×2000 / (8×2000 + 30000) = 0.348
j = 1 − k/3 = 1 − 0.348/3 = 0.884

Maximum moment

M = 4 × 400 / 8 = 200 k·ft = 2,400,000 lb·in

Required bd² (concrete governs)

bd² = 2M / (k·j·fc) = 2×200×12000 / (0.348 × 0.884 × 2000)
bd² = 7,801 in³ → Try b=12": d = √(7801/12) = 25.5"

Required steel area As

As = M / (fs·j·d) = 200×12000 / (30000 × 0.884 × 25.5)
As = 3.55 in²

Choose bars

#6 bars (A=0.44 in²): 3.55/0.44 = 8.07 → Use 9 #6 bars

#8 bars (A=0.79 in²): 3.55/0.79 = 4.49 → Use 5 #8 bars

Total h ≈ 25.5 + 1.5 + 0.5 ≈ 28" → use h = 28" beam, d ≈ 25.5"
