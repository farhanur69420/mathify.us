Reinforced Concrete:
Working Stress Design

Concrete is brilliant in compression but breaks easily in tension. Steel does the opposite. Together, they form one of the most powerful structural materials in the world. This chapter explains exactly how and why — from atoms to equations.

Why Reinforced Concrete?

Concrete alone is like a piece of chalk — strong if you squeeze it, but snaps the moment you try to bend or pull it. That's where steel steps in.

🪨

Concrete in Compression

Concrete is an artificial stone made of cement, sand, water, and air. Like stone, it handles compression extremely well — loads pushing down on it.

f'c = 3,000 – 5,000 psi
⚠️

Concrete in Tension

Concrete is terrible at handling tension (pulling forces). Its tensile strength is roughly 1/10 of its compressive strength. It cracks easily.

ft ≈ f'c / 10
🔩

Steel Takes the Tension

Steel bars (rebars) are placed in the tension zone of a concrete beam. They carry the tensile forces that concrete cannot handle. Perfect teamwork.

fy = 60 ksi (Grade 60)
🏗️

The RC Team

In a beam bending under load: the top is in compression (concrete handles it), the bottom is in tension (steel handles it). Neither could do it alone.

n = Es/Ec ≈ 8
Plain English: Imagine bending a ruler. The top edge gets shorter (compression) and the bottom edge gets longer (tension). Now imagine the ruler is concrete — it will snap on the tension side. RC puts steel exactly where the snapping would happen.

A Brief History

ANCIENT ROME

Pozzolana Cement

Romans used volcanic ash (pozzolana) mixed with lime to make a primitive concrete. Used in the Pantheon dome.

1796 — ENGLAND

Portland Cement

Joseph Aspdin patents Portland cement — the same cement used in concrete worldwide today.

1832

First Concrete House

Plain concrete used for buildings. Immediately clear it struggles with tension.

1867 — THE KEY DATE

Reinforced Concrete: Joseph Monier

French gardener Joseph Monier patents iron-reinforced concrete flower pots. The idea spreads rapidly to beams, slabs, and eventually skyscrapers.

01 — MATERIAL PROPERTIES

Concrete & Steel: The Numbers

Before designing anything, engineers need to know exactly how their materials behave under stress. These are the key numbers for every RC calculation.

Stress–Strain Curves

CONCRETE — COMPRESSIVE STRESS-STRAIN
STEEL — STRESS-STRAIN (ELASTIC-PERFECTLY PLASTIC)
Concrete (non-linear, peak at f'c)
Steel (linear then flat at fy)
WSD range (linear only)
Key insight: In Working Stress Design (WSD), we only use the linear portion of both curves — where stress is directly proportional to strain. This keeps the math simple and adds a safety margin below the non-linear zone.

MODULUS OF ELASTICITY — CONCRETE (ACI)
\[ E_c = 57{,}000\sqrt{f'_c} \quad \text{(psi)} \]
For f'c = 5,000 psi: Ec = 57,000 × √5000 ≈ 4 × 10⁶ psi
MODULUS OF RUPTURE (Cracking Stress)
\[ f_r = 7.5\sqrt{f'_c} \quad \text{(psi)} \]
This is the tensile stress at which concrete first cracks. Below this → section is uncracked. Above this → section cracks and steel activates.

Modular Ratio Calculator

The modular ratio n tells us how much stiffer steel is compared to concrete. It's the key to transforming a two-material problem into a single-material one.

MODULAR RATIO
\[ n = \frac{E_s}{E_c} = \frac{29 \times 10^6}{57{,}000\sqrt{f'_c}} \]
n tells us: "1 in² of steel is equivalent to n in² of concrete." Typically n ≈ 6–10 for common concrete strengths.

02 — BENDING STRESS

The Flexure Formula & Its Limits

When a beam bends, stresses vary linearly from top to bottom — compression on one side, tension on the other. But there's a critical catch for RC beams.

BENDING (FLEXURE) STRESS FORMULA
\[ \sigma = \frac{M \cdot y}{I} \]
σ = bending stress at distance y from neutral axis
M = bending moment at the section
y = distance from neutral axis to the point of interest
I = moment of inertia of the cross-section
⚠️ RC Limitation: This formula was derived for materials that are homogeneous (same throughout) and isotropic (same in all directions). Reinforced concrete is neither — it's two different materials. We need special treatment to use this formula for RC.

Solution: The Transformed Section

Since steel is n times stiffer than concrete, we can replace the steel bars with an equivalent area of concrete. This creates a single-material "transformed section" we can analyze normally.

ACTUAL RC SECTION
→
TRANSFORMED SECTION
TRANSFORMED STEEL AREA
\[ A_{s,\text{transformed}} = n \cdot A_s \]
Replace each steel bar with n times its area in concrete. Now treat the whole section as concrete and apply σ = My/I normally.
FINDING THE NEUTRAL AXIS (Transformed Section)

Take moments of area about the neutral axis and set equal to zero:

\[ b \cdot \bar{y} \cdot \frac{\bar{y}}{2} = n \cdot A_s \cdot (d - \bar{y}) \]
where ȳ = depth to neutral axis from top, d = effective depth to steel centroid
03 — THREE STAGES OF LOADING

From Zero Load to Collapse

An RC beam doesn't fail suddenly. It goes through three distinct stages as load increases — and understanding each stage is the foundation of all RC analysis.

STAGE I

Section Uncracked

Tensile stress in concrete is below the modulus of rupture (fr). The full cross-section — concrete and steel together — resists the bending moment. Linear elastic behavior.

fct < fr = 7.5√f'c
STAGE II

Section Cracked (Elastic)

Tensile stress exceeds fr — concrete cracks in the tension zone. Concrete in tension is ignored (fct = 0). Steel carries all tension. This is the WSD working range.

fs < fy , fc < ½f'c
STAGE III

Ultimate / Failure

Steel yields (reaches fy) or concrete crushes (εu = 0.003). Non-linear behavior. This is the basis of Strength Design (USD), not covered in WSD.

fs = fy or εc = 0.003

Stage I → Stage II: The Cracking Moment

The transition from Stage I to Stage II happens when the tensile stress at the bottom of the beam equals the modulus of rupture fr. This defines the cracking moment Mcr.

Check tensile stress using bending formula

Using the uncracked transformed section, compute the tensile stress at the extreme bottom fiber:

\( f_{ct} = \frac{M \cdot y_{bottom}}{I_{transformed}} \)

Compare to modulus of rupture

If fct < fr: Section is uncracked → use Stage I analysis.
If fct > fr: Section has cracked → use Stage II (cracked section) analysis.

\( f_r = 7.5\sqrt{f'_c} \quad \text{(psi)} \)

After cracking: set concrete tension to zero

In Stage II, we simply assume concrete cannot carry any tension at all. The analysis changes: only the compression zone above the neutral axis carries compression, and steel carries all tension.

After cracking: fct = 0 (concrete tension ignored)
04 — CRACKED SECTION ANALYSIS (WSD)

The k & j Method

Once a section is cracked (Stage II), we need a different approach. The cracked section analysis introduces two key dimensionless parameters: k (neutral axis ratio) and j (moment arm ratio).

CRACKED SECTION — FORCES
COMPRESSION FORCE (in concrete)
\[ C = \frac{1}{2} f_c \cdot b \cdot kd \]
TENSION FORCE (in steel)
\[ T = A_s \cdot f_s \]
MOMENT EQUILIBRIUM
\[ M = T \cdot jd = A_s \cdot f_s \cdot jd \] \[ M = C \cdot jd = \tfrac{1}{2} f_c \cdot b \cdot kd \cdot jd \]
Key idea: Equilibrium tells us C = T (no net horizontal force). This gives us the relationship between the concrete stress fc and steel stress fs. With a known moment M, we can then find both stresses.

Here's where k and j come from — derived from first principles using strain compatibility and force equilibrium.

Define the reinforcement ratio ρ (rho)

ρ is the ratio of steel area to effective concrete area. It tells us how much steel we have relative to the beam size.

\( \rho = \frac{A_s}{b \cdot d} \)

Force equilibrium: C = T

Setting compression force equal to tension force:

\( \frac{1}{2} f_c \cdot b \cdot kd = A_s \cdot f_s \quad \Rightarrow \quad \rho \cdot f_s = \frac{1}{2} f_c \cdot k \)

Strain compatibility: steel and concrete strains are compatible

The strain diagram is linear. Using similar triangles and the modular ratio n:

\( \frac{f_s / E_s}{d - kd} = \frac{f_c / E_c}{kd} \quad \Rightarrow \quad k = \frac{n \cdot f_c}{n \cdot f_c + f_s} \)

Solve the quadratic for k

Combining steps 2 and 3 and substituting ρn = p·n:

\( k^2 + 2\rho n \cdot k - 2\rho n = 0 \)
\( \boxed{k = \sqrt{(\rho n)^2 + 2\rho n} - \rho n} \)

The moment arm ratio j

The internal moment arm (distance between C and T) is jd, where:

\( \boxed{j = 1 - \frac{k}{3}} \)
This comes from the centroid of the triangular stress block being at kd/3 from the top.
STRESSES — SOLVING FOR fc AND fs
\[ f_s = \frac{M}{A_s \cdot j \cdot d} \qquad f_c = \frac{2M}{k \cdot j \cdot b \cdot d^2} \]

Q.1.A Simply Supported Beam — Find all stresses

GIVEN

Beam span:L = 20 ft, simply supported
Load:w = 4 k/ft (uniform)
Width:b = 12"
Total height:h = 18"
Steel:6 #6 bars
Concrete:f'c = 4,000 psi
Steel:fy = 60 ksi

Compute section properties

Steel area and effective depth:

As = 6 × 0.44 = 2.64 in²
d = h − cover − stirrup − ½bar = 18 − 1.5 − 0 − 6/8 − 0.5 = 15.25"

Compute reinforcement ratio and modular ratio

ρ = As/(bd) = 2.64/(12 × 15.25) = 0.0144
n = Es/Ec = 29×10⁶ / (57,000√4000) = 8.0
ρn = 0.0144 × 8 = 0.115

Find k and j

k = √(ρn)² + 2ρn − ρn = √(0.115² + 0.230) − 0.115 = 0.378
j = 1 − k/3 = 1 − 0.378/3 = 0.874

Maximum bending moment

M = wL²/8 = 4 × 20² / 8 = 200 k·ft = 2,400 k·in

Steel stress fs

fs = M / (As·j·d) = 2400 / (2.64 × 0.874 × 15.25)
fs = 68.2 ksi

Concrete stress fc

fc = 2M / (k·j·b·d²) = 2×2400 / (0.378 × 0.874 × 12 × 15.25²)
fc = 5.2 ksi = 5,200 psi
Interpretation: Steel stress is 68.2 ksi, which exceeds fy = 60 ksi — the beam as designed would have the steel yielding! This indicates the section needs to be redesigned (larger beam or more steel) for safe WSD. This is exactly the scenario that motivates the design problem in Q.1(b).
05 — WORKING STRESS DESIGN (WSD)

Designing an RC Beam

WSD limits stresses to safe fractions of the material strengths. The allowable stresses are set at half of failure — giving a safety factor of 2.

🔵

Allowable Concrete Stress

In WSD, concrete compression is limited to half its compressive strength to stay in the linear range.

fc,allow = ½ f'c
🟡

Allowable Steel Stress

Steel stress is limited to half its yield strength — ensuring we stay well within the elastic range.

fs,allow = ½ fy
WSD DESIGN EQUATIONS

From the moment equation M = C·jd = T·jd, solving for required bd² and As:

\[ bd^2 = \frac{2M}{k \cdot j \cdot f_c} \qquad A_s = \frac{M}{f_s \cdot j \cdot d} \]
where fc = ½f'c and fs = ½fy are the allowable stresses
Q.1.B Design cross-section for w = 4 k/ft, L = 20 ft

GIVEN

Load:w = 4 k/ft, L = 20 ft
f'c:4,000 psi → fc = ½(4000) = 2,000 psi
fy:60,000 psi → fs = ½(60,000) = 30,000 psi

Compute k from allowable stresses

Using the stress ratio method with n = 8:

k = n·fc / (n·fc + fs) = 8×2000 / (8×2000 + 30000) = 0.348
j = 1 − k/3 = 1 − 0.348/3 = 0.884

Maximum moment

M = wL²/8 = 4 × 400 / 8 = 200 k·ft = 2,400,000 lb·in

Required bd² (from concrete stress governs)

bd² = 2M / (k·j·fc) = 2×200×1000×12 / (0.348 × 0.884 × 2000)
bd² = 7,801 in³

Try b = 12": d² = 7801/12 = 650 → d = 25.5". Total h ≈ 27–28"

Required steel area As

As = M / (fs·j·d) = 200×1000×12 / (30000 × 0.884 × 25.5)
As = 3.55 in²

Choose bars

Try #5 bars (A = 0.31 in²): Number = 3.55/0.31 = 11.45 → Use 12 #5 bars

Try #6 bars (A = 0.44 in²): Number = 3.55/0.44 = 8.07 → Use 9 #6 bars

Final selection: verify d is consistent, iterate if needed
