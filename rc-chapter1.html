01 — LEC 1 — FOUNDATION

What Even Is Reinforced Concrete?

Imagine squeezing a chalk stick — it holds. Now try bending it — it snaps. Concrete behaves exactly like that. Steel does the opposite. Put them together, and you have a near-perfect building material.

MATERIAL COMPARISON — Click to test each material under tension and compression
🪨

Concrete in Compression

Strong. Like stone, it handles loads pushing on it extremely well. This is its superpower.

f'c = 3,000 – 5,000 psi
⚠️

Concrete in Tension

Terrible. Concrete's tensile strength is only about 1/10 of its compressive strength. It cracks almost immediately.

fr ≈ 7.5√f'c
🔩

Steel Takes Tension

Steel bars (rebars) go where concrete fails — in the tension zone at the bottom of a beam. Perfect teamwork.

fy = 60,000 psi
🔗

Bond = The Secret

Concrete grips steel so tightly they deform together. This "strain compatibility" is the core assumption of all RC design.

εs = εc (at same depth)
Historical note: Romans used volcanic ash (pozzolana) to make early concrete 2,000 years ago. Modern Portland cement was patented in 1824. The idea of putting iron bars inside concrete came from Joseph Monier in 1867 — a French gardener who was trying to make stronger flowerpots.

🔴 Concrete Curve — What to Notice

The curve rises non-linearly — concrete gets softer as stress increases. It peaks at f'c (the "prime-c" you'll see everywhere), then fails suddenly. No yielding. No warning. This is why we don't rely on concrete for tension.

🔵 Steel Curve — What to Notice

Steel has two clear phases: a straight elastic zone (returns to original shape), then a yield plateau — it stretches without extra force. This warning behavior is what makes RC beams safe. We design for this zone.

What is "f'c"? The apostrophe means "compressive strength at 28 days." Concrete gets stronger as it cures. Engineers test cylinders at exactly 28 days to measure its strength. The prime (') is just notation — it means "we're talking about compression."
02b — LEC 2 — MIX DESIGN

What Goes Into Concrete?

Concrete is a carefully tuned recipe of four ingredients. The ratio matters enormously — get it wrong and you get weak, porous, or unworkable concrete.

INTERACTIVE MIX DESIGNER — Adjust w/c ratio to see strength effect
w/c RATIO: 0.45 f'c ≈ 4,800 psi — Excellent
🏔️

Coarse Aggregate

Crushed stone or gravel. Makes up 70-75% of the volume. Standard size: ¾ inch (downgraded). Provides bulk and compressive strength.

CA = ¾" standard
🏖️

Fine Aggregate (Sand)

Fills the gaps between coarse aggregate particles. Improves workability and reduces voids.

Mix ratio 1:1.5:3
🔷

Cement + Water

Cement is the adhesive. Water activates the chemical reaction (hydration). Less water = stronger concrete, but harder to pour.

w/c = 0.40–0.60

Key Geometry Rules

Cover: 1.5" minimum — why? ▼
Cover is the concrete between the steel bar and the outside face of the beam. It protects steel from fire and corrosion. Minimum 1.5" for beams. 2.5–3" for below ground or offshore structures.

d = h − cover − d_stirrup − db/2

Where db is the bar diameter. This is your effective depth — the distance from the top to the center of the steel.
Minimum beam width: b ≥ 12" ▼
Per BNBC, beams must be at least 12 inches wide. Use even numbers: 12", 14", 16", 18", 20"... Bars must have at least 1" clear space between them (to let concrete flow through during pouring).
Reinforcement ratio ρ (rho) — the key variable ▼
ρ = As / (b × d) — ratio of steel area to beam cross-sectional area. Low ρ = ductile (gives warning before failing). High ρ = brittle failure. Code limits: ρ_min = 200/fy and ρ_max = 0.75 × ρ_balanced.

05 — LEC 6 — BEHAVIOR UNDER LOAD

The Three Stages of Loading

As load increases from zero to failure, a concrete beam goes through three distinct stages. Understanding these stages IS RC design. The simulation below shows all three.

3-STAGE BEAM SIMULATOR — Increase load to watch each stage
LOAD LEVEL: 0% STAGE I — UNCRACKED Entire section resists load

Stage I — Uncracked

Load is small. The entire beam cross-section (concrete + steel) resists bending. Concrete hasn't cracked yet. Tensile stress at the bottom is below the modulus of rupture fr = 7.5√f'c.

f_ct < fr

Stage II — Cracked Elastic

Concrete at the bottom has cracked. Steel takes over and carries all the tension. Concrete in compression is still linear (elastic). This is the Working Stress Design (WSD) range.

f_ct > fr, steel elastic

Stage III — Ultimate

Steel yields (or concrete crushes). Nonlinear behavior. This is the basis for Ultimate Strength Design (USD / LRFD). The beam is near failure.

Steel yields: fs = fy
WSD operates in Stage II. Working Stress Design keeps stresses below the elastic limit — steel stays elastic, concrete compression stays linear. This is safer (conservative) and simpler to calculate. Stage III is for Ultimate Strength Design (USD), covered in a separate chapter.
06 — LEC 7–8 — WORKING STRESS DESIGN

The Math — Built From Scratch

Every term in every formula comes from a physical observation. We'll build each equation from first principles. No memorization — just understanding.

CRACKED SECTION — Stage II beam with stress and strain diagrams
What is BENDING STRESS?▼
When a beam bends under load, one side gets squeezed (compression) and the other gets stretched (tension). The intensity of this squeezing/stretching is called bending stress (σ or f).

Key insight: Stress varies linearly with distance from the neutral axis. Zero stress at the NA. Maximum stress at the top (compression) and bottom (tension).
What is STRAIN COMPATIBILITY?▼
Since steel and concrete are bonded together, they must deform the same amount at the same location. This means: εs = εc at the level of the steel. Strain (ε) is just deformation per unit length — how much something stretches relative to its original size.

From this: fs/Es = fc/Ec × (d-kd)/kd... which leads to the relationship between concrete and steel stresses.
What is FORCE EQUILIBRIUM?▼
The total compression force (C) in the concrete must equal the total tension force (T) in the steel. The beam isn't moving — so ΣF = 0.

C = T → (1/2) × fc × k × d × b = As × fs

This gives us the equation to find the neutral axis position k.
NEUTRAL AXIS FINDER — Adjust ρ and n to find k
ρ (rho): 0.012 n = Es/Ec: 8 k = —, j = —
NEUTRAL AXIS FORMULA (derived from C = T and strain compatibility)
k = √[ (ρn)² + 2ρn ] − ρn
kThe fraction of the beam depth occupied by the compression zone. If k = 0.35, the neutral axis is at 0.35×d from the top. Below the NA, concrete is cracked and ignored in Stage II. = neutral axis depth ratio (NA depth = k×d)
ρReinforcement ratio = As/(b×d). Typical values: 0.005 to 0.02. Higher ρ means more steel and a deeper neutral axis. = As/(b×d) — steel to beam area ratio
nModular ratio = Es/Ec. How many times stiffer steel is compared to concrete. For f'c=4000 psi: Ec=3,640,000 psi, so n = 29,000,000/3,640,000 ≈ 8. = Es/Ec — modular ratio (≈ 8 for f'c = 4,000 psi)
MOMENT ARM RATIO
j = 1 − k/3
jThe fraction of d that represents the distance between the compression resultant (at k×d/3 from top) and the tension resultant (at d). This is the lever arm of the internal couple. = moment arm ratio (internal lever arm = j×d)
The compression force acts at k×d/3 from the top. So the lever arm is d − k×d/3 = d(1 − k/3) = j×d.
INTERNAL FORCES — See how the moment is resisted
The key insight: A beam resists bending with an internal couple — compression block on top and tension steel at bottom. The moment M = force × lever arm. We can solve for stress by working backwards from M.
MOMENT = FORCE × LEVER ARM (for both the C block and T rod)
M = C × jd = T × jd
C = (1/2) × fc × k×d × b (triangular compression block)
T = As × fs (steel carries all tension)
SOLVE FOR STEEL STRESS fs
fs = M / (As × j × d)
Rearranging T = M → As×fs = M/jd → fs = M/(As×j×d). Units: if M is in k·in, As in in², d in in → fs in ksi.
SOLVE FOR CONCRETE STRESS fc
fc = 2M / (k × j × b × d²)
Rearranging C = M/jd → (1/2)×fc×k×d×b = M/jd → fc = 2M/(k×j×b×d²). Units: if M in k·in, b and d in in → fc in ksi.
ALLOWABLE STRESS CHECK — Live analysis of the beam
Moment M (k·ft): 75 k·ft
WSD Allowable Stresses (Safety Factor = 2):
Concrete: fc,allow = f'c/2 = 0.45×f'c (e.g., 0.45×4000 = 1,800 psi)
Steel: fs,allow = fy/2 = 0.5×fy (e.g., 0.5×60,000 = 30,000 psi)
If actual stress > allowable → ❌ Unsafe. Must redesign.
WORKED PROBLEM Q.0 Is this section cracked? Uncracked transformed section analysis
GIVEN
L = 20 ft, w = 0.75 k/ft (simply supported)
b = 12", h = 18", 3-#7 bars, f'c = 4,000 psi, fy = 60 ksi

Find effective depth d and compute properties

d = 18 − 1.5 − 0.375 − 0.875/2 = 15.69" ≈ 16.1" Ec = 57,000√4000 = 3,605,000 psi → n = 29,000,000/3,605,000 ≈ 8 As = 3 × 0.60 = 1.80 in²

Find neutral axis of uncracked transformed section (ȳ from top)

ȳ = [b×h×(h/2) + (n−1)×As×d] / [b×h + (n−1)×As] = [12×18×9 + 7×1.80×16.1] / [12×18 + 7×1.80] = [1944 + 203] / [216 + 12.6] = 2147/228.6 = 9.39"

Compute moment of inertia (transformed, uncracked) Itr

Itr = b×h³/12 + b×h×(ȳ − h/2)² + (n−1)×As×(d−ȳ)² = 5832 + 216×(9.39−9)² + 12.6×(16.1−9.39)² = 5832 + 32.8 + 568 = 6,433 in⁴

Compute applied moment and tensile stress at bottom

M = wL²/8 = 0.75×20²/8 = 37.5 k·ft = 450 k·in f_ct = M×(h−ȳ)/Itr = 450×(18−9.39)/6433 = 450×8.61/6433 = 602 psi

Compare to modulus of rupture fr — cracking limit

fr = 7.5×√4000 = 7.5×63.25 = 474 psi f_ct = 602 psi > fr = 474 psi → SECTION IS CRACKED
Conclusion: Even at this relatively small load (0.75 k/ft), the concrete has cracked. This happens in almost every real beam. We must now use the cracked section (k&j) method for Stage II analysis.
Analysis vs Design: Analysis = given a beam, find stresses. Design = given loads, find beam dimensions. WSD lets us do both with the same equations.
WSD DESIGN — Find required bd² and As
bd² = 2M / (k × j × fc,allow)    As = M / (fs,allow × j × d)
Step 1: Choose f'c and fy → compute k and j (they depend only on ρ and n)
Step 2: Use bd² formula to find cross-section size. Pick b, solve for d.
Step 3: Use As formula to find required steel area. Pick bar size and number.
```
WORKED PROBLEM Q.1 WSD Analysis + Design for a Simply Supported Beam
GIVEN
L = 20 ft, w = 4 k/ft (DL+LL), f'c = 3,000 psi, fy = 60 ksi
Part A: Analyze existing beam (b=12", h=20", 6-#6 bars)
Part B: Design a new beam from scratch

Part A — Analysis

Compute geometry and k, j

d = 20 − 1.5 − 0.375 − 0.75/2 = 17.75" ≈ 17.75" ``` As = 6 × 0.44 = 2.64 in², n = 9 (for f’c=3000) ρ = 2.64/(12×17.75) = 0.0124, ρn = 0.112 k = √(0.112² + 2×0.112) − 0.112 = √(0.0125+0.224) − 0.112 = 0.474−0.112 = 0.362 j = 1 − 0.362/3 = 0.879

Compute applied moment and actual stresses

M = 4×20²/8 = 200 k·ft = 2400 k·in fs = 2400 / (2.64 × 0.879 × 17.75) = 2400/41.2 = 58.3 ksi fc = 2×2400 / (0.362 × 0.879 × 12 × 17.75²) = 4800/1208 = 3.97 ksi

Check against allowable stresses

fc,allow = 0.45×3000 = 1350 psi = 1.35 ksi → fc=3.97 > 1.35 ❌ UNSAFE fs,allow = 0.5×60 = 30 ksi → fs=58.3 > 30 ksi ❌ UNSAFE → Beam FAILS in both concrete and steel. Must redesign.
```

Part B — Design

Compute balanced k and j for given materials

fc,allow = 1.35 ksi, fs,allow = 30 ksi, n = 9 ``` k_bal = n×fc / (n×fc + fs) = 9×1.35/(9×1.35+30) = 12.15/42.15 = 0.288 j_bal = 1 − 0.288/3 = 0.904

Find required bd² (concrete stress controls)

bd² = 2M/(k×j×fc) = 2×2400/(0.288×0.904×1.35) = 4800/0.3516 = 13,652 in³ Choose b=14”: d² = 13652/14 = 975 → d = 31.2” → use d=32” h = d + 2.375 ≈ 34.4” → use h = 36”

Find required As (steel stress controls)

As = M/(fs×j×d) = 2400/(30×0.904×32) = 2400/867.8 = 2.77 in² Use 5-#7 bars: As = 5×0.60 = 3.0 in² ✅ Check: 3.0 > 2.77 ✅ → SAFE
