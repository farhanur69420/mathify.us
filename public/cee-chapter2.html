§ 2.1

Introduction

⚡ Why Does This Matter?

Concrete is not a single material — it's a composite system. Understanding each ingredient and how they interact is the difference between a structure that lasts 100 years and one that fails in 10. Everything in this course depends on understanding what's inside your concrete.

🧠 The Big Picture Analogy

Think of concrete like a fruitcake. The cake batter (cement paste) holds everything together, while the fruits and nuts (aggregates) provide bulk and economy. The recipe — how much of each ingredient, how long you bake it — determines whether it's delicious or inedible. Steel reinforcement is like a wire mesh baked inside: invisible but essential for holding the whole thing together when it's stressed.

Concrete System Overview

Technical Overview

Concrete is a composite material consisting of cement paste (cement + water) as binder, and aggregates (fine sand + coarse gravel/rock) as filler. The strength and durability depend on:

  • The type and quality of cement
  • The water-cement (w/c) ratio
  • The gradation and cleanliness of aggregates
  • Curing conditions (temperature, moisture, time)
  • Use of admixtures to modify properties

Reinforced concrete adds steel bars (rebars) to carry tensile forces that concrete cannot resist on its own.

§ 2.2

Cement

⚡ Why Does This Matter?

Cement is the chemical engine of concrete. Choosing the wrong type of cement — or mixing it poorly — can cause a bridge deck to crack within months, a slab to heave from sulfate attack, or a pour to freeze solid overnight. The type of cement you specify will affect strength gain rate, heat generation, durability, and cost.

🧠 Analogy: Epoxy Glue

Portland cement works like a two-part epoxy. Dry cement is Part A. Water is Part B. When mixed, a chemical reaction called hydration starts — and unlike epoxy, this reaction can keep going for years! The cement particles grow microscopic crystals that interlock and harden, locking aggregates in place. This is why concrete gets stronger over time, not weaker.

Why Heat of Hydration Matters

The hydration reaction releases heat. In a thin slab, this heat escapes easily. In a massive dam or thick mat foundation, the core can reach 160°F+ while the surface stays cool — creating temperature gradients that crack the concrete before it even carries load.

Solution: Type IV cement (low heat), pre-cooling aggregates, or mixing ice water. The Hoover Dam used special cements and embedded cooling pipes to manage this.

💡

Key Fact: Portland cement accounts for about 65–75% of concrete's cost by weight. Replacing some cement with fly ash or slag (supplementary cementitious materials) saves money and often improves long-term durability.

§ 2.3

Aggregates

⚡ Why Does This Matter?

Aggregates make up 60–75% of concrete's volume. They're not just cheap filler — they directly control strength, stiffness, thermal expansion, and freeze-thaw resistance. A poorly graded aggregate wastes cement paste, weakens the mix, and can cause pumping problems on site.

🧠 Analogy: Packing Marbles in a Jar

Imagine filling a jar with marbles of all the same size — there are large gaps between them. Now add some smaller marbles — they fill the gaps. Add sand — fills more gaps. Add water — fills the rest. Well-graded aggregate uses this same principle: a range of particle sizes packs more efficiently, requiring less cement paste to fill the voids, which means a stronger, cheaper, less-shrinkage-prone concrete.

Aggregate Gradation Visualization

Technical Requirements

  • Fine aggregate (sand): Particles under ¼ in. (No. 4 sieve). Must be clean — clay or organic matter weakens the cement-aggregate bond.
  • Coarse aggregate: Gravel or crushed rock, typically ⅜ to 1½ in. Larger sizes require less water (lower w/c ratio → stronger concrete), but can't fit between rebars.
  • Maximum aggregate size is limited to ¾ of the clear space between rebars, or 1/5 of the narrowest form dimension — whichever is smaller.
  • Alkali-silica reaction (ASR): Certain reactive aggregates expand when in contact with alkaline cement, causing internal cracking. Use low-alkali cement or pozzolanic SCMs to prevent it.
Quick Check: A wall section is 8 inches wide with rebars spaced at 2 inches clear. What is the maximum allowable aggregate size?
The ACI Code limits max aggregate size to the smallest of: ¾ × clear spacing = 1.5 in, or ⅕ of minimum form dimension = 1.6 in. Take the smaller: 1.5 inches.
§ 2.4

Proportioning and Mixing Concrete

⚡ Why Does This Matter?

The water-cement (w/c) ratio is arguably the single most important number in concrete design. It directly controls compressive strength, permeability, and durability. Too much water makes weak, porous concrete. Too little makes it unworkable on site. Getting this balance right is everything.

🧠 Analogy: Baking Bread

Too much water in bread dough → dense, gummy loaf with weak structure. Too little → you can't even knead it. The right amount gives you a workable dough that rises properly and bakes into a strong loaf. In concrete, the "baking" is curing, and the strength of the loaf is measured at 28 days.

ACI Mix Design Process

  • Step 1 — Target Strength: Required f'c plus a safety margin. ACI 318 adds ~1,200 psi for variability.
  • Step 2 — w/c ratio: Selected from tables based on target strength and exposure class.
  • Step 3 — Water content: Determined by desired slump and max aggregate size.
  • Step 4 — Cement content: = Water ÷ (w/c ratio)
  • Step 5 — Aggregate proportions: Coarse and fine amounts to fill remaining volume.
  • Step 6 — Trial batches: Make cylinders and test at 7 and 28 days to verify.
⚠️

Critical Rule: Never add water to concrete on-site to make it flow easier. This increases the w/c ratio, drops strength, and creates a structure that may not meet code requirements. Use superplasticizers instead.

§ 2.5

Conveying, Placing, Compacting & Curing

⚡ Why Does This Matter?

You can design a perfect mix on paper and still end up with weak, honeycombed concrete if it's placed badly. Segregation, trapped air pockets, and poor curing all destroy concrete quality. This is where the lab meets the field.

🧠 Analogy: Pouring Pancake Batter

Conveying = pouring the batter into the pan carefully (don't splatter). Compacting = tapping the pan to release air bubbles. Curing = keeping the stove at just the right temperature long enough — if you rush it by cranking up the heat (or letting the surface dry too fast), the pancake looks done but the center is wrong.

§ 2.6

Quality Control

⚡ Why Does This Matter?

Concrete strength is inherently variable — even from the same plant, on the same day. Quality control defines how we measure that variability and set strength targets high enough that almost no batch falls below the minimum required by the code. This protects lives.

🧠 Analogy: Manufacturing Bolts

A factory making bolts rated at 10 kN doesn't make every bolt exactly at 10 kN — they aim higher (say, 12 kN average) to ensure almost none fall below the minimum. Concrete works the same way. The required average strength (f'cr) is always higher than the specified strength (f'c) to account for normal variation.

Strength Distribution — Why We Need a Margin Above f'c
400 psi

ACI Required Average Strength

When enough test data exists (≥30 cylinders), ACI 318 requires:

f'cr = f'c + 1.34σ (no result more than 500 psi below f'c)
f'cr = f'c + 2.33σ − 500 (no average of 3 consecutive below f'c)

Where σ is the standard deviation of test results. Less data → ACI uses prescribed margins.

Slump test measures workability (how easily concrete flows). Standard: 3–4 in. for beams.

Cylinder test (6×12 in. or 4×8 in.) is the primary strength verification method — cast on site, tested at 28 days.

Quick Check: A contractor's concrete has f'c = 4,000 psi specified and a standard deviation σ = 600 psi. What is the required average strength f'cr (using the larger of the two ACI equations)?
Eq 1: 4000 + 1.34×600 = 4,804 psi. Eq 2: 4000 + 2.33×600 − 500 = 4,898 psi. Use the larger: 4,898 psi.
§ 2.7

Admixtures

⚡ Why Does This Matter?

Admixtures are the pharmacology of concrete. Small doses — often less than 1% by cement weight — can dramatically change workability, setting time, strength, and durability. Modern high-rises, long-span bridges, and underwater structures wouldn't be possible without them.

🧠 Analogy: Food Additives

Preservatives extend shelf life (retarders = slow setting for hot weather or long hauls). Leavening agents make dough rise (air-entraining agents add tiny bubbles for freeze-thaw resistance). Food coloring changes appearance (pigments). Emulsifiers let water and oil mix (water reducers let water and cement mix better without adding more water).

⚡
Accelerators

Speed up hydration. Useful in cold weather or fast-track work. Calcium chloride is cheap but causes steel corrosion — use non-chloride type in reinforced concrete.

🐌
Retarders

Slow setting time. Used in hot weather or for large pours that take many hours. Prevents cold joints between lifts.

❄️
Air-Entraining

Creates billions of microscopic air bubbles (~3–6% air). These act as pressure-relief valves for freezing water — essential for pavements in cold climates.

🧪
Water Reducers / Superplasticizers

Reduce water needed for same workability by 12–30%. Same strength at lower w/c ratio — or same w/c with better flow for pumping and congested rebar.

🛡️
Pozzolans (SCMs)

Fly ash, silica fume, slag. React with calcium hydroxide to form additional binding compounds. Improve durability, reduce permeability, lower heat of hydration.

💧
Shrinkage Reducers

Reduce drying shrinkage and cracking in slabs. Critical for flat floor slabs where crack control is important for aesthetics and serviceability.

§ 2.8

Properties in Compression

⚡ Why Does This Matter?

This is the most important section in Chapter 2. The stress-strain curve of concrete in compression is the foundation of every beam, column, and slab design you'll ever do. The shape of this curve determines how a structure fails — and whether that failure is sudden or gradual.

🧠 Analogy: Crushing a Foam Cup

At first, a foam cup compresses uniformly and bounces back — elastic behavior. Keep pushing and you reach the cup's peak capacity. Then it starts to crush and fold unevenly — plastic behavior. Finally it collapses. Concrete follows a similar path: linear → nonlinear → peak → descending branch. Unlike the cup, concrete gives almost no warning before peak — which is why we design columns to be ductile (wrapped in spirals) so they can absorb energy without sudden collapse.

Key Relationships from the Curve

  • Elastic Modulus (Ec): The initial slope. ACI formula: Ec = 33 × wc^1.5 × √f'c (for normal weight concrete ≈ 57,000√f'c psi)
  • Peak strain: ε₀ ≈ 0.002 (strain at maximum stress) — same regardless of strength!
  • Ultimate strain: εu = 0.003 — the ACI-assumed limit for design at the extreme compression fiber
  • Higher f'c → steeper curve, more brittle. High-strength concrete fails more suddenly.
  • Confinement (spiral reinforcement) extends the descending branch enormously — creating ductile columns that don't collapse suddenly in earthquakes.

§ 2.9

Properties in Tension

⚡ Why Does This Matter?

Concrete is only about 8–15% as strong in tension as in compression. This is the fundamental reason we add steel reinforcement — steel handles the tension, concrete handles the compression. Understanding tensile strength also predicts cracking, which affects serviceability, durability, and how loads are redistributed.

🧠 Analogy: Chalk Stick

Hold a piece of chalk and press it from both ends — hard to break (compression). Now try to pull it from both ends — it snaps easily (tension). Concrete is the same: great under squeezing, terrible under pulling. This is why unreinforced concrete buildings are rare — any bending creates tension on one face, and it cracks. Steel bars placed on the tension side carry those pulling forces instead.

Three Ways to Measure Tensile Strength of Concrete

Tensile Strength Estimates

Direct tension tests are unreliable (gripping concrete is hard). Instead we use:

  • Modulus of Rupture (fr): Third-point bending test. fr = 7.5λ√f'c (psi). Measures flexural tensile strength — highest estimate.
  • Splitting Tensile Strength (fct): Brazil test — cylinder on its side. fct ≈ 6.7√f'c (psi). Most common indirect test.
  • Direct Tension: Difficult to test, rarely done. ≈ 4√f'c (psi) estimated.

The ACI Code conservatively assumes cracked concrete carries zero tension in flexural design — all tension goes to steel.

fr = 7.5λ√f'c (psi) — Modulus of Rupture (ACI 318)
💡

For a 4,000 psi concrete: f'c = 4,000 → Compression capacity = 4,000 psi. Tensile capacity (fr) = 7.5 × √4000 = 474 psi. That's only 12% of the compressive strength!

§ 2.10

Strength under Combined Stress

⚡ Why Does This Matter?

Real structures don't experience pure compression or pure tension — they experience combinations of both simultaneously. A beam in bending also has shear. A column has compression plus bending. Understanding how concrete fails under combined stresses determines how we design stirrups, spiral ties, and interaction diagrams for columns.

🧠 Analogy: Arm Wrestling + Push-Up Simultaneously

You're stronger at each individually than you are at both at once. Concrete is the same — if you're using up compressive capacity in one direction, there's less capacity available in another. A biaxial compression state actually increases concrete's strength slightly. But compression in one direction + tension in another reduces the tensile capacity significantly.

Biaxial Failure Envelope for Concrete

Key Failure States

  • Biaxial tension: Both directions pulling — weakest state. Strength barely reaches uniaxial tensile strength.
  • Tension + Compression: Tensile strength reduced as compressive stress increases. Critical for shear design.
  • Biaxial compression: Slightly stronger than uniaxial — compression in one direction helps resist cracking in the other (up to ~16% increase).
  • Application: This is why shear (diagonal tension) cracks form at 45° — the principal tensile stress exceeds the tensile strength of concrete.
§ 2.11

Shrinkage and Temperature Effects

⚡ Why Does This Matter?

Concrete wants to shrink as it dries. But it's attached to other elements — foundations, walls, rebar — that resist this shrinkage. The result? Tensile stresses build up inside and concrete cracks, even before any load is applied. Shrinkage cracking damages waterproofing, looks bad, and can reduce structural durability. Understanding it is key to placing reinforcement correctly and spacing control joints.

🧠 Analogy: Drying Clay

Wet clay is soft and flexible. As it dries, it wants to shrink. If you dry a flat clay slab attached to a rigid base, it can't shrink freely — so it cracks. Concrete does the same. The "clay" is the cement paste; the "rigid base" is the aggregate, the steel bars, or the adjacent structure. Shrinkage reinforcement doesn't prevent cracking — it forces many fine cracks instead of a few wide, damaging ones.

Shrinkage Strain Development Over Time

Types of Volume Change

  • Plastic shrinkage: Occurs in first few hours after placement — surface dries faster than interior. Prevent with evaporation retarder sprays and wind breaks.
  • Drying shrinkage: Long-term moisture loss. Ultimate value ≈ 400–800 × 10⁻⁶ (0.04–0.08%). High water content, smaller aggregates, and low humidity all increase shrinkage.
  • Creep: Under sustained load, concrete deforms slowly over time (separate from elastic deformation). Creep strain can be 1–3× the elastic strain. Critical for long-term deflection predictions.
  • Thermal: Coefficient of thermal expansion ≈ 5–6 × 10⁻⁶ /°F. A 100 ft concrete member changes ≈ 0.7 in. over a 100°F temperature swing. This is why expansion joints are required every 200–300 ft.
εsh (ultimate) ≈ 400–800 × 10⁻⁶ | Creep coefficient Cc ≈ 1.0–3.0 × elastic strain
⚠️

Designer Trap: ACI requires minimum shrinkage reinforcement even in slabs that aren't structurally needed for load. For slabs: As,min = 0.0018 × bh (Grade 60 steel). Without it, wide cracks open up and the slab deteriorates rapidly.

§ 2.12

High-Strength Concrete

⚡ Why Does This Matter?

High-strength concrete (HSC), with f'c above 6,000 psi (and often reaching 15,000–20,000 psi in tall buildings), enables slimmer columns, longer spans, and reduced dead loads. But HSC behaves differently — it's more brittle, has a higher modulus, and requires modified design equations. Modern skyscrapers like the Burj Khalifa wouldn't be possible without it.

🧠 Analogy: Carbon Fiber vs. Aluminum

Carbon fiber is much stronger than aluminum per unit weight — but it's also brittle. It doesn't bend before breaking; it shatters. HSC behaves similarly: you get more strength, but you must design specifically for the reduced ductility or the structure can fail catastrophically under overload without warning.

💪
Achieved By

Low w/c ratio (≤0.35), silica fume, superplasticizers, high-quality aggregates, careful curing. Sometimes reactive powder concretes exceed 50,000 psi!

📐
Modified Behavior

Peak strain ε₀ stays near 0.002–0.003 but descending branch becomes very steep. ACI uses modified stress block parameters (β₁ reduced) for f'c > 4,000 psi.

🏗️
Design Adjustments

β₁ = 0.85 − 0.05(f'c − 4000)/1000 for f'c > 4,000 psi, min 0.65. Affects neutral axis depth calculations in beam design.

🏙️
Applications

High-rise columns, bridge piers, offshore platforms. Chicago's Water Tower Place (1976) used 9,000 psi — revolutionary at the time. Now 15,000+ psi is routine.

β₁ = 0.85 − 0.05 × (f'c − 4000) / 1000 ≥ 0.65
§ 2.13

Reinforcing Steels for Concrete

⚡ Why Does This Matter?

Steel is what makes concrete a structural material worth designing with. Without steel, concrete is just a very expensive rock — strong in compression but dangerously brittle in tension. The steel-concrete team works because: (1) steel has 100× concrete's tensile strength, (2) their thermal expansion coefficients are nearly identical (so the bond doesn't break with temperature changes), and (3) the alkaline environment in concrete protects steel from corrosion.

🧠 Analogy: Reinforced Fishing Rod

A glass fishing rod is stiff and strong under compression (when you're storing it vertically) but can snap under bending. Wrap it in carbon fiber and now it bends elastically under load without breaking. The fibers (like rebar) carry the tensile stress along the outer surface. The glass matrix (like concrete) carries the compression. Together they're far stronger than either alone.

Steel vs. Concrete Stress-Strain Curves — Why They're Perfect Partners

Why This Partnership Works

  • Bond: Deformed bars have ribs that mechanically interlock with concrete. Smooth bars (old buildings) rely only on friction — much weaker bond.
  • Thermal compatibility: Steel: α = 6.5 × 10⁻⁶/°F. Concrete: α = 5.5 × 10⁻⁶/°F. Close enough that temperature changes don't break the bond.
  • Corrosion protection: High pH of concrete (pH ≈ 12–13) forms a passive oxide layer on steel. This breaks down if chlorides penetrate (coastal environments, deicing salts) → rebar corrosion → concrete spalling.
  • Elastic modulus: Es = 29,000,000 psi for all grades of steel — much higher than concrete (≈3–5 million psi), so steel carries loads efficiently even in small cross-sections.
§ 2.14

Reinforcing Bars

⚡ Why Does This Matter?

Bar size selection directly affects spacing, concrete cover, development lengths, and cost. The wrong bar size can make a beam impossible to detail properly — too large bars mean insufficient cover or spacing, failing code requirements. Understanding the bar designation system is essential for reading structural drawings.

🧠 Analogy: Pipe Sizes

A "#8 bar" doesn't mean 8 inches — it means 8/8 = 1 inch diameter. Each bar number corresponds to eighths of an inch in nominal diameter. A #4 bar = 4/8 = ½ inch. A #10 bar = 10/8 = 1¼ inch. This numbering system makes it easy to specify bars on drawings and order from the fabricator.

§ 2.15

Welded Wire Reinforcement

⚡ Why Does This Matter?

For slabs-on-grade, pavements, and precast elements, placing and tying individual rebars is expensive and time-consuming. Welded wire reinforcement (WWR) — prefabricated sheets or rolls of wire — provides two-directional reinforcement in a single placement operation, reducing labor cost and improving consistency.

🧠 Analogy: Window Screen vs. Individual Wires

You could stretch individual horizontal and vertical wires across a window frame and tie each intersection by hand — or you could just use a prefabricated screen. WWR is the prefabricated screen of concrete reinforcement: faster to install, more consistent spacing, and the welds at intersections provide excellent mechanical anchorage.

Designation System

WWR is designated by: Spacing × Spacing — Wire Size × Wire Size

Example: 6×6 — W2.9×W2.9 means 6-in. spacing each way, wires of 0.029 in² area each.

  • W = plain wire, D = deformed wire
  • Number after W or D = wire cross-sectional area in hundredths of in²
  • Yield strength: 65,000 psi (plain) to 75,000 psi (deformed)
  • Widely used in slabs-on-grade, floor slabs, precast panels, and pipe
§ 2.16

Prestressing Steels

⚡ Why Does This Matter?

Regular rebar works by stretching — it's passive. Prestressing steel is actively tensioned before (or after) concrete is cast, creating built-in compressive forces that counteract future tension from loads. This allows much longer spans, thinner sections, and crack-free concrete under service conditions. Prestressed concrete is used in virtually every major bridge and parking garage you've seen.

🧠 Analogy: Squeezing a Row of Books

Lay 10 books side by side and try to lift the row as a beam — they fall apart. Now squeeze the ends hard with your hands (like a prestressing force) and suddenly you can lift the whole row as a unit. The compression from your hands prevents the books from separating at the bottom under bending. Prestress applies the same principle: precompressing the concrete before loads arrive means bending loads first have to overcome the precompression before any tension (and cracking) begins.

Quick Check: Why can't we use Grade 60 rebar for prestressing?
Prestress losses from shrinkage, creep, and relaxation are on the order of 20–35 ksi. On a 60 ksi yield bar, that's a 30–50% loss — you'd barely have any prestress left. Strand at 270 ksi has the same absolute losses but they represent only ~10% of the initial stress, leaving ≥90% still effective.
§ 2.17

Fiber Reinforcement

⚡ Why Does This Matter?

Traditional rebar resists cracks after they form — but only at the bar locations. Fibers distributed throughout the mix resist cracking everywhere, providing crack control at the micro-level, improving toughness (energy absorption), and enabling thinner sections. Fiber-reinforced concrete is increasingly used in slabs, tunnels, and shotcrete applications.

🧠 Analogy: Papier-Mâché vs. Plain Paper

A single sheet of paper tears easily. Papier-mâché — layers of paper in all orientations — is far tougher and can absorb energy without catastrophic failure. Fibers in concrete do the same thing at the microscale: they bridge across forming cracks, requiring much more energy to propagate the crack through the matrix.

🔩
Steel Fibers

Hooked-end or crimped steel, 0.5–2 in. long. Dramatically increase toughness and post-crack ductility. Used in precast concrete, industrial floors, tunnel linings.

🧵
Synthetic Fibers

Polypropylene, nylon, or polyethylene. Primarily control plastic shrinkage cracking. Don't significantly increase strength but reduce early-age cracking.

🌿
Glass Fibers

Used in thin panels and architectural concrete. Must be alkali-resistant (AR glass) — plain glass deteriorates in the high-pH concrete environment.

⚫
Carbon Fibers

Extremely high strength and stiffness, very expensive. Used in research and special applications. UHPC (ultra-high performance concrete) uses steel fibers at 2% by volume.

Ultra-High Performance Concrete (UHPC)

The frontier of fiber reinforcement. UHPC combines: steel fibers (1–3% by volume) + very low w/c (≤0.20) + silica fume + superplasticizers → compressive strengths of 20,000–50,000 psi with significant tensile capacity (800–1,200 psi, vs. 400 psi for normal concrete).

Applications: ductal bridge decks, thin precast facades, blast-resistant structures, connection joints in segmental construction.

✅ Chapter 2 — The Big Ideas

1. Concrete = cement paste + aggregates. The w/c ratio is the master variable controlling strength, durability, and permeability.

2. Concrete is strong in compression (f'c), weak in tension (≈ 10× weaker). Steel reinforcement solves this — placed where tension occurs.

3. The stress-strain curve shape determines ductility. Higher strength → more brittle → more care needed in design.

4. Shrinkage and creep cause long-term deformations that are often larger than immediate elastic deflections — ignore them at your peril.

5. Prestressing uses high-strength steel to precompress concrete, enabling crack-free, long-span structures impossible with normal rebar.

6. Admixtures, fibers, and supplementary materials are engineering tools — use them deliberately to solve specific problems.
