Reinforced Concrete Design

Concrete is strong in compression but brittle in tension. Steel is the opposite. Together they form one of the most versatile structural materials ever invented.

```
The Big Idea

Imagine trying to build a bridge using only chalk. Chalk can take a lot of squeezing (compression) but snap it and it breaks instantly — it has no tensile strength. Plain concrete is the same. Now imagine a rubber band — it stretches brilliantly but collapses under compression. Steel is that rubber band. Reinforced concrete combines both: concrete handles compression, steel handles tension.

💡 Intuition First
When you bend a beam, the top gets squeezed (compressed) and the bottom gets stretched (tensioned). Concrete handles the top. Steel bars at the bottom handle the tension. That's the whole idea.
🔬 Live Simulation — Beam Bending Stress
Drag the load slider to see how compression (blue) and tension (red) change
Load ↓ 50%
🧱
Concrete

Compressive strength f'c = 3,000–6,000 psi typically. Cheap, moldable, fire-resistant. Essentially zero tensile strength.

🔩
Steel Rebar

Yield strength fy = 40,000–60,000 psi. Handles tension brilliantly. Perfect bond with concrete is assumed.

📖 Why does concrete crack without steel?
A plain concrete beam under a central load — where and why does it crack?
1
A load pushes down at the center of the beam.
2
The beam bends. The top fibers are compressed (shortened). The bottom fibers are stretched (tensioned).
3
Concrete tensile strength ≈ only 10–15% of compressive strength. The bottom cracks almost immediately.
4
Solution: Place steel bars near the bottom. Steel carries the tension. Concrete carries the compression. Beam survives.
Concrete Properties

Concrete is a mix of cement, water, fine aggregate (sand), and coarse aggregate (gravel). The water-cement ratio is the single most important parameter — lower w/c = stronger concrete.

Key Property
Compressive strength: f'c — tested on 6"×12" cylinders at 28 days
Modulus of elasticity: Ec = 57,000 √f'c (psi) — ACI 318

The stress-strain curve of concrete is NOT linear — it rises, peaks at about 0.85f'c, then softens and crushes at a strain of about 0.003.

📈 Stress-Strain Curve
Concrete (blue) vs Steel (orange) — note the difference in behavior
Steel Properties

Rebar has a beautifully simple stress-strain curve: linear elastic until yield, then it flows (yields) at constant stress. This is called elastic-perfectly plastic behavior and it's what makes WSD analysis possible.

Steel Properties
Yield strength: fy = 40,000 or 60,000 psi (Grade 40 or 60)
Modulus of elasticity: Es = 29,000,000 psi (29,000 ksi) — constant
Modular ratio: n = Es / Ec ≈ 7–10
How RC Beams Fail (Step by Step)

As load increases on an RC beam, it goes through three distinct stages. Understanding these stages is the entire foundation of RC design.

⚡ 3 Stages of Loading — Interactive
Stage 1 — Uncracked
Load is low. The whole section (concrete + steel) resists bending. Stress is linear everywhere. Full cross-section is effective.
Stage 2 — Cracked
Tensile stress exceeds concrete's limit. Cracks form at the bottom. Now ONLY the steel resists tension. Neutral axis shifts up. This is the WSD design stage.
Stage 3 — Failure
Either steel yields (ductile, preferred — warning before failure) or concrete crushes (brittle, avoided). Under-reinforced sections fail by steel yielding first.
🎯 Why WSD uses Stage 2
Working Stress Design assumes the beam is always at Stage 2 — cracked but still elastic. We keep stresses below allowed values so we never reach failure. Think of it as designing with a big safety buffer between "working load" and "collapse."
The k and j Method

For a cracked, elastic RC beam, we need to find where the neutral axis is (k) and how far apart the concrete compression and steel tension forces act (j). These two dimensionless ratios unlock all the WSD calculations.

💡 What k and j mean physically
k = fraction of beam depth to the neutral axis from the top (e.g., k = 0.35 means NA is 35% down). j = fraction of depth representing the moment arm between compression and tension forces. Higher j = more efficient beam.
Key Formulas — WSD Cracked Section
ρ = As / (b·d)    (reinforcement ratio)

k = √[(ρn)² + 2ρn] − ρn

j = 1 − k/3

Concrete stress: fc = 2M / (k·j·b·d²)

Steel stress: fs = M / (As·j·d)

✏️ Worked Example — WSD Analysis
A singly reinforced beam has b = 12", d = 21.5", As = 3.0 in², f'c = 3000 psi, fy = 60,000 psi, n = 9. Find k, j, and check stresses under M = 120 kip·ft.
1
Find ρ: ρ = As/(bd) = 3.0/(12 × 21.5) = 0.01163
2
Find ρn: ρn = 0.01163 × 9 = 0.1047
3
Find k: k = √[(0.1047)² + 2(0.1047)] − 0.1047 = √[0.0110 + 0.2094] − 0.1047 = √0.2204 − 0.1047 = 0.4695 − 0.1047 = 0.365
4
Find j: j = 1 − k/3 = 1 − 0.365/3 = 0.878
5
Steel stress: fs = M/(As·j·d) = (120×12,000)/(3.0 × 0.878 × 21.5) = 1,440,000/56.6 = 25,441 psi ✓ (allowable = 24,000 psi — slightly over, may need to resize)
6
Concrete stress: fc = 2M/(k·j·b·d²) = 2(1,440,000)/(0.365×0.878×12×462.25) = 2,880,000/1,775 = 1,623 psi ✓ (allowable = 0.45f'c = 1,350 psi — also over, beam too small)
Designing a Beam from Scratch

Analysis asks: "given this beam, what are the stresses?" Design asks: "given the load, what beam do I need?" The process is similar but reversed — we choose balanced design as a starting point.

🎯 The Design Strategy
A balanced design reaches the allowable concrete AND steel stresses simultaneously. This is the most efficient use of both materials. In practice, we often use slightly under-reinforced designs (steel governs) since steel failure gives warning.
Design Formulas
Balanced ρ: first find kb = n·fc,allow / (n·fc,allow + fs,allow)

Required bd² = M / (Rb)   where Rb = ½ fc,allow · kb · jb

Required As = M / (fs,allow · j · d)
✏️ Full Design Example
Design a singly reinforced rectangular beam for M = 200 kip·ft. Use f'c = 3000 psi, fy = 60,000 psi. Allowables: fc = 1350 psi, fs = 24,000 psi, n = 9.
1
Balanced k: kb = (9×1350)/(9×1350 + 24000) = 12150/36150 = 0.336
2
Balanced j: jb = 1 − 0.336/3 = 0.888
3
Resistance coefficient: Rb = ½(1350)(0.336)(0.888) = 201.8 psi
4
Required bd²: bd² = M/Rb = (200×12,000)/201.8 = 11,892 in³
5
Choose dimensions: Try b = 14", then d² = 11892/14 = 849 → d = 29.1". Round to d = 30". Full depth h ≈ 32.5" (accounting for cover + stirrups).
6
Required As: As = M/(fs·j·d) = (200×12,000)/(24,000 × 0.888 × 30) = 2,400,000/639,360 = 3.75 in²
7
Select bars: Use 4 × #9 bars (4 × 1.00 = 4.00 in²) ✓ — slightly above required, conservative is fine.
📝 Practice Problems
P1. A beam has b = 10", d = 18", As = 2.5 in², n = 9. Find k and j.
ρ = 2.5/(10×18) = 0.01389  |  ρn = 0.1250  |  k = √(0.01563 + 0.2500) − 0.1250 = 0.5063 − 0.1250 = 0.381  |  j = 1 − 0.381/3 = 0.873
P2. For the beam above, find steel and concrete stresses if M = 80 kip·ft.
fs = M/(As·j·d) = (80×12,000)/(2.5×0.873×18) = 960,000/39,285 = 24,436 psi
fc = 2M/(k·j·b·d²) = 1,920,000/(0.381×0.873×10×324) = 1,920,000/1,077 = 1,782 psi

Traffic Analysis & Design

How do engineers design roads, signals, and intersections to move people as efficiently as possible? It all comes down to flow, capacity, and time.

```
The Fundamental Triangle

Three variables describe traffic flow and they're always connected:

q — Flow (veh/hr)
How many vehicles pass a point per hour. Like water flow rate in a pipe.
k — Density (veh/mi)
How packed the road is — vehicles per mile of roadway.
u — Speed (mph)
Average speed of vehicles at a given point and time.
Fundamental Equation of Traffic Flow
q = k × u

Flow = Density × Speed

At max density (jam density kj): flow = 0, speed = 0
At max flow (capacity qmax): optimal speed uo = uf/2 (Greenshields)

Level of Service (LOS)

LOS is a letter grade (A through F) that describes how well a road or intersection is operating. Think of it like a school report card for traffic.

💡 Quick Mental Model
LOS A = empty highway, perfect freedom. LOS F = bumper-to-bumper gridlock. Engineers design for LOS C or D — acceptable conditions under expected loads.
LOS V/C Ratio Description
A≤ 0.60Free flow, no congestion, drivers choose own speeds.
B≤ 0.70Stable flow, minor traffic interactions, comfortable.
C≤ 0.80Stable but drivers start noticing other vehicles. Typical design standard.
D≤ 0.90High density, speed begins to drop, some delay acceptable.
E≤ 1.00At or near capacity. Any incident causes breakdown.
F> 1.00Forced flow, breakdown. Demand exceeds capacity. Queues form.
Signal Timing Basics

A traffic signal cycle has to give green time to competing movements. The goal is to minimize total delay for all users. Key terms:

Cycle Length C
Total time for all phases to complete once. Typical: 60–120 seconds.
Green Time G
Time allocated to each phase for vehicles to pass.
Lost Time L
Time lost at phase start/end due to driver reaction and yellow clearance. Typically 3–4 sec/phase.
Saturation Flow s
Max flow rate if the light were always green (~1800 veh/hr/lane).
Webster's Optimal Cycle Length
Co = (1.5L + 5) / (1 − Σyi)

where yi = qi/si (volume-to-saturation ratio for critical movement in each phase)
and L = total lost time per cycle = n × ℓ (n = number of phases)
🚦 Signal Cycle Animation
✏️ Worked Example — Cycle Length Design
A 2-phase signal has: Phase 1 critical volume = 800 vph, Phase 2 = 600 vph. Saturation flow = 1800 vph/lane. Lost time per phase = 4s. Find optimal cycle length and green splits.
1
Critical flow ratios: y₁ = 800/1800 = 0.444    y₂ = 600/1800 = 0.333
2
Σy = 0.444 + 0.333 = 0.778    Total lost time L = 2 × 4 = 8s
3
Optimal cycle: Co = (1.5×8 + 5)/(1 − 0.778) = 17/0.222 = 76.6 s → Use 80s
4
Effective green time: Geff = C − L = 80 − 8 = 72s
5
Green splits: G₁ = 72 × (0.444/0.778) = 41s    G₂ = 72 × (0.333/0.778) = 31s
The 4-Step Travel Demand Model

Before building a road, engineers predict how many people will use it. The 4-step model is the standard tool — applied in sequence, each step feeding the next.

Step 1 — Trip Generation
How many trips does each zone produce and attract? Based on land use, population, employment density.
Step 2 — Trip Distribution
Which zones attract which zones' trips? Gravity model: more trips between large zones that are close together.
Step 3 — Mode Split
How do people travel? Car, transit, bike, walk? Logit model uses utility (cost, time) to predict choices.
Step 4 — Network Assignment
Which route does each trip take? User equilibrium: no driver can reduce their travel time by switching routes.
Gravity Model (Trip Distribution)
Tij = Pi · Aj · Fij · Kij / Σ(Aj · Fij · Kij)

where Fij = 1/tij² (friction factor — inverse square of travel time)
Pi = productions from zone i, Aj = attractions to zone j
Logit Mode Split Model
P(mode m) = e^(U_m) / Σe^(U_k)

Utility U = β₀ + β₁·(travel time) + β₂·(cost) + ...
Calibrated from survey data. Negative coefficients for time and cost.
📝 Practice Problems
P1. A 3-phase signal has y₁=0.35, y₂=0.40, y₃=0.20, lost time = 3s/phase. Find optimal C.
L = 3×3 = 9s  |  Σy = 0.35+0.40+0.20 = 0.95  |  Co = (1.5×9+5)/(1−0.95) = 18.5/0.05 = 370s (This is very high — Σy is too close to 1.0, intersection is near/over capacity and needs extra lanes or signal optimization)
P2. Two travel modes have utilities U_car = −0.05(20) − 0.04(5) = −1.20 and U_transit = −0.05(40) − 0.04(1) = −2.04. Find probability of choosing car.
P(car) = e^(−1.20) / (e^(−1.20) + e^(−2.04)) = 0.301 / (0.301 + 0.130) = 0.301/0.431 = 69.8%
